**1. Followings are true concerning with the pattern of growth**

<details>
<summary>A. Rate of growth is maximum in utero</summary>
`True` - Fetal life → rapid cellular hyperplasia → highest growth rate compared to postnatal periods.
</details>

<details>
<summary>B. Growth of nervous system is most rapid between 2 to 5 years</summary>
~~False~~ - Neural tissue → maximal growth during intrauterine and first 2 years → not between 2–5 years.
</details>

<details>
<summary>C. Tonsils of an adult is bigger than a child of 10 years old</summary>
~~False~~ - Lymphoid tissue → physiological hypertrophy in childhood (around 5–10 years) → involutes in adulthood.
</details>

<details>
<summary>D. Rapid genital growth is seen at the onset of puberty</summary>
`True` - Pubertal hormones (testosterone, estrogen) → stimulate rapid growth of genitalia at puberty onset.
</details>

<details>
<summary>E. Upper segment and lower segment ratio is highest in the newborn period</summary>
`True` - Newborn → relatively short legs compared to trunk → higher US:LS ratio → decreases with age.
</details>

**5. Followings are the features of pathological jaundice**

<details>
<summary>A. Rate of rise of serum bilirubin is more than 5 mg/dl/hour</summary>
~~False~~ - Pathological if rise > 5 mg/dl per day, not per hour.
</details>

<details>
<summary>B. Clinical jaundice is detected within 24-48 hours of life</summary>
`True` - Appearance within first 24 hrs → always pathological.
</details>

<details>
<summary>C. Total serum bilirubin elevation is more than 5 mg/dl in term infants</summary>
~~False~~ - Physiological jaundice can exceed 5 mg/dl; pathological if > 12–15 mg/dl in term.
</details>

<details>
<summary>D. Direct bilirubin more than 2 mg % of any time</summary>
`True` - Conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (>2 mg/dl) → pathological (cholestasis, sepsis).
</details>

<details>
<summary>E. It peaks on 5th day and disappears by 7th day in term infants</summary>
~~False~~ - This describes physiological jaundice → not pathological.
</details>
