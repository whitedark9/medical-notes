# Acute Pulmonary Oedema

> A 65-year-old male chronic smoker presenting with sudden onset of breathlessness, coughing
> up of pink frothy sputum. On examination, the patient cannot lie flat, blood pressure is
> 180/100 mmHg, pulse rate is 120/min, oxygen saturation is 92% on air and crepitations all
> over the lung fields. How will you assess and manage this patient?

## 1. Immediate Assessment – ABCDE (Medicine approach)

**A – Airway**

- Ensure airway patency; frothy sputum may require suction.
- Prepare for advanced airway if GCS drops or oxygenation fails.

**B – Breathing**

- Monitor RR, SpO₂, use of accessory muscles.
- Auscultation: widespread fine crackles.
- ABG: hypoxaemia, possible respiratory alkalosis initially → later respiratory acidosis.

**C – Circulation**

- BP: markedly elevated (180/100 mmHg) → suggests hypertensive emergency as precipitant.
- HR: tachycardia 120/min; check for arrhythmia.
- ECG: myocardial infarction, LVH, arrhythmias.

**D – Disability**

- GCS (hypoxia can impair consciousness).
- Blood glucose.

**E – Exposure**

- Signs of heart failure: raised JVP, peripheral oedema.
- Assess for precipitating factors: acute coronary syndrome, arrhythmias, valve disease, fluid overload.

## 2. Investigations

**For Diagnosis**

- CXR → bat’s wing pattern, upper lobe diversion, Kerley B lines.
- ABG → hypoxaemia ± hypercapnia.
- ECG → MI, arrhythmias.
- BNP/NT-proBNP → supports diagnosis of heart failure.

**For Causes**

- Cardiac enzymes (troponin) → MI.
- Echocardiography → LV function, valvular lesions.
- Thyroid function → thyrotoxic cardiomyopathy.
- U\&E → renal impairment, electrolyte abnormalities.

**For Complications**

- Repeat ABG → monitor gas exchange.
- Continuous cardiac monitoring → arrhythmias.
- Renal function monitoring after diuretics.

## 3. Emergency Management – Medicine Sequence (Davidson’s)

**General Principles**: Treat **hypoxia, reduce preload, reduce afterload, treat cause**.

### Position

- Sit patient upright with legs dependent → ↓ venous return.

### Oxygen & Ventilatory Support

- High-flow oxygen (target SpO₂ >94%, unless COPD → 88–92%).
- CPAP/NIV early if persistent hypoxia despite oxygen.

### Pharmacological

1. **Diuretics**

   - IV furosemide 40–80 mg slow IV bolus → venodilation (rapid) + diuresis (delayed).

2. **Vasodilators** (if SBP > 100 mmHg)

   - Sublingual GTN spray/tablet → repeat after 5–10 min if BP allows.
   - IV GTN infusion if persistent hypertension.

3. **Morphine** (2.5–5 mg IV slowly)

   - Relieves anxiety, reduces preload (venodilation). Use with caution in elderly/CO₂ retainers.

4. **BP Control**

   - If hypertensive crisis → nitrates or sodium nitroprusside (in ICU setting).

5. **Inotropes** (if cardiogenic shock & low BP)

   - Dobutamine / noradrenaline (critical care only).

### Identify & Treat Cause

- Acute MI → ACS protocol (antiplatelet, anticoagulants, reperfusion).
- Arrhythmia → rhythm or rate control.
- Severe valve lesion → urgent cardiology review.

## 4. Pathophysiology Sequence

Chronic smoking / underlying LV dysfunction + hypertensive crisis →
↑ Afterload → LV cannot eject effectively →
↑ LV end-diastolic pressure →
↑ Pulmonary venous pressure →
Fluid transudation into interstitium & alveoli →
↓ Gas exchange → hypoxaemia → pink frothy sputum.

## 5. Disposition

- Admit to coronary care or HDU.
- Continuous monitoring until stable.
- Once acute episode resolved → optimise long-term HF management (ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, diuretics, risk factor control, smoking cessation).
