# HIV-TB

> A 24-year-old driver presenting with fever, cough, dyspnea and weight loss for 2 months.
> Discuss the possible differential diagnoses?

## 1. Possible Differential Diagnoses

### Respiratory Causes

1. **Pulmonary Tuberculosis (TB)**

   - Most likely in TB-endemic areas.
   - Chronic productive cough, fever (often evening rise), night sweats, weight loss.
   - Haemoptysis may occur.

2. **Chronic Bacterial Pneumonia**

   - May persist if incompletely treated or drug-resistant.
   - Often caused by _S. aureus_, _Klebsiella_, _Pseudomonas_.

3. **Bronchiectasis**

   - Recurrent infections, copious purulent sputum, possible haemoptysis.

4. **Lung Abscess**

   - Post-pneumonia or aspiration; foul-smelling sputum.

5. **Chronic Fungal Infection**

   - _Aspergillus_, histoplasmosis; often post-TB cavity or immunocompromised host.

### Systemic/Other Causes

6. **HIV with Opportunistic Pulmonary Infection**

   - TB (pulmonary or extrapulmonary)
   - PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia)
   - Cryptococcosis, cytomegalovirus pneumonitis.

7. **Malignancy (lymphoma, rare primary lung cancer at this age)**
8. **Sarcoidosis** – systemic granulomatous disease.

## 2. Assessment

### A. History

- **Symptoms:**

  - Fever pattern (intermittent, prolonged, evening rise → TB)
  - Cough: dry vs productive, sputum colour, blood-streaked or haemoptysis
  - Dyspnoea: onset & progression
  - Weight loss: amount, duration
  - Night sweats, chest pain

- **Risk factors:**

  - TB contact, HIV risk behaviour, immunosuppressive drugs, chronic illnesses, smoking, alcohol/drug use

- **Past history:**

  - Prior TB or pneumonia, childhood lung diseases

- **Occupation/environment:** dust exposure, silica, asbestos.

### B. Physical Examination

- **General:** pallor, cachexia, clubbing, fever, lymphadenopathy
- **Respiratory:**

  - Asymmetrical chest movement, tracheal deviation
  - Dull percussion, bronchial breathing, crackles, wheeze

- **Other systems:** hepatosplenomegaly, skin lesions (sarcoidosis, Kaposi’s), oral thrush (HIV).

### C. Investigations

#### For Diagnosis

- **CBC** → anaemia of chronic disease, leukocytosis
- **ESR/CRP** → raised in infection/inflammation
- **Chest X-ray** → cavities, infiltrates, hilar lymphadenopathy, miliary pattern, mass, effusion
- **Sputum for AFB** ×3 (Ziehl–Neelsen) ± **GeneXpert MTB/RIF**
- **Sputum culture** (bacterial, fungal)

#### For Causes

- **HIV serology** (ELISA/rapid test)
- **CD4 count**, **HIV viral load** (if HIV+)
- **Mantoux** / IGRA
- **CT chest** for detailed lung pathology
- **Bronchoscopy** + **BAL** if sputum negative but suspicion high
- **Lymph node biopsy** if enlarged

#### For Complications

- **ABG** (if hypoxia suspected)
- **LFT/RFT** (baseline before anti-TB & ART drugs)
- **Hepatitis B/C** screening (before ART)

## 3. Special Case – HIV–TB Co-infection

**Clinical Features**

- More extrapulmonary TB (lymphadenitis, pleural, meningeal)
- Pulmonary TB may have:

  - Less cavitation
  - Lower/middle lobe involvement
  - Sometimes normal CXR

- Rapid deterioration without treatment

**Additional Investigations**

- HIV-positive patients need:

  - CD4 count (severity, timing of ART)
  - HIV viral load
  - Screening for other OIs: cryptococcal antigen, PCP testing (if indicated)

## 4. Management Overview

### A. Pulmonary TB (HIV– or HIV+)

- **Drug-sensitive TB regimen** (DOTS preferred):

  - **Intensive phase (2 months):** `HRZE` (`isoniazid`, `rifampicin`, `pyrazinamide`, `ethambutol`)
  - **Continuation phase (4 months):** `HR`

- Monitor LFTs & vision (`ethambutol`).

### B. HIV–TB Co-infection

- **Start TB treatment first** (reduces bacterial load before immune restoration).
- **Start ART in all TB–HIV patients**, timing by CD4 count:

  - **CD4 < 50 cells/µL:** Start ART **within 2 weeks** of TB therapy.
  - **CD4 ≥ 50 cells/µL:** Start ART **within 8 weeks**.

- **Preferred ART regimen with `rifampicin`:** `Efavirenz` + `2 NRTIs` (e.g., `tenofovir` + `lamivudine`).
- **Preventive measures:** `Cotrimoxazole` prophylaxis, nutritional support.
- **Watch for IRIS:** Continue both ART & TB drugs; `steroids` if severe.

### C. Other Causes – Brief Treatments

| Diagnosis                       | Main Treatment                                                            |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Chronic bacterial pneumonia** | Targeted antibiotics per culture; treat underlying cause                  |
| **Bronchiectasis flare**        | Antibiotics, chest physiotherapy, vaccination                             |
| **Lung abscess**                | IV antibiotics (e.g., `clindamycin`), drainage if needed                  |
| **Fungal infection**            | `Itraconazole`/`voriconazole` or `amphotericin B` (depending on organism) |
| **PCP**                         | High-dose `co-trimoxazole` ± `corticosteroids` if severe                  |
| **Malignancy**                  | Chemo/radiotherapy/surgery per stage                                      |
| **Sarcoidosis**                 | Observation or corticosteroids if symptomatic                             |

## 5. Pathophysiology Link (TB–HIV)

HIV → ↓ CD4 → impaired granuloma formation → rapid TB progression
TB → ↑ HIV replication due to immune activation → faster HIV progression.
