# Portal Hypertension

> HVPG = WHVP - FHVP

- HVPG = Hepatic Venous Pressure Gradient
- WHVP = Wedged Hepatic Venous Pressure
- FHVP = Free Hepatic Venous Pressure

> Normal HVPG = 5-6 mmHg<br/>In portal hypertension, > 10 mmHg<br/>Risk of variceal bleeding, > 12 mmHg

## Causes

1. Pre-hepatic pre-sinusoidal
   - Portal vein thrombosis due to sepsis (umbilical, portal pyaemia) or procoagulopathy or secondary to cirrhosis
   - Abdominal trauma including surgery
1. Intrahepatic pre-sinusoidal
   - **Schistosomiasis** (Most common)
   - Congenital hepatic fibrosis
   - Drugs
   - Vinyl chloride
   - Sarcoidosis
1. Sinusoidal
   - **Cirrhosis** (Most common)
   - Polycystic liver disease
   - Nodular regenerative hyperplasia
   - Metastatic malignant disease
1. Intrahepatic post-sinusoidal
   - Veno-occlusive disease
1. Post-hepatic post-sinusoidal
   - Budd-Chiari syndrome

## Complications of Portal Hypertension

- Variceal bleeding: Oesophageal, gastric, other
- Congestive gastropathy
- Hypersplenism
- Ascites
- Iron deficiency anaemia
- Renal failure
- Hepatic encephalopathy

## Investigations

- Clinical
- **Full blod count**: Thrombocytopenia[^1], leucopenia
- **Endoscopy**: Most useful to detect gastro-oesophageal varices
- **Ultrasonography**: To detect splenomegaly and collateral vessels
- **CT and magnetic resonance angiography**: To detect portal vein clot and hepatic vein patency

## Management

> Management of portal hypertension is largely focused on the prevention and/or control of variceal haemorrhage.

- `Non-selective β-blocker` (`propranolol` or `nadolol`) (to reduce portal venous pressure) OR
- `Non-cardioselective vasodiating β-blocker` (`carvedilol`)

- Source of bleeding should always be confirmed by endoscopy
- Prophylactic `broad-spectrum antibiotics` (IV `cephalosporin` or `piperacillin/tazobactam`) (to prevent sepsis)

#### Pharmacological Management

- Vasoconstrictor `Terlipressin` 2 mg IV 4 times daily until bleeding stops, then 1 mg 4 times daily for up to 72 hours
- Caution: _Severe ischaemic heart disease or peripheral vascular disease_
- Alternative: `Octreotide`

#### Non-Pharmacological Management

1. **Variceal ligation ('banding')**
   - Regular follow-up endoscopy is required to identify and treat any recurrence of varices
   - Prophylactic `PPI` for acid suppression (to reduce the risk of secondary bleeding from banding-induced ulceration)
   - Less effective in gastric varices
   - Endotracheal intubation during active bleeding (to reduce risk of pulmonary aspiration)
1. **Balloon tamponade**
   - If early endoscopic therapy is not available or is unsucessful
   - Endotracheal intubation (to reduce risk of pulmonary aspiration)
1. **TIPSS** (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Stent Shunt)
   - A stent placed between the portal vein and hepatic vein within the liver to provide a portosystemic shunt and reduce portal pressure
   - Effective for both oesophageal and gastric varices
   - Complications: Hepatice encephalopathy occurs in up to a third of pateints following TIPSS
1. **Portosystemic shunt surgery**
   - For patient with good liver function
1. **Oesophageal transection**
   - As a last resort

### Secondary Prevention of Variceal Bleeding

- `β-blockers` (to prevent recurrent variceal bleeding)
- Enter an `oesophageal banding programme` with repeated sessions of therapy at 2-4-week intervals until the varices are obliterated
