# Diabetes Ketoacidosis (DKA)

> DKA is an acute metabolic complication (medical emergency) of diabetes that is a consequence of **absolute** insulin deficiency.

## Precipitating Factors

1. First presentation of type 1 diabetes mellitus
1. **Infection**: Pneumonia, UTI
1. **Underlying illness**\*: Acute myocardial infarction, Cardiovascular disease, Pancreatitis
1. **Physiological stress**\*: Surgery, trauma, pregnancy
1. **Insulin deficiency**: Due to discontinuation or inadequate delivery of insulin

## Complications

1. Hypoglycaemia
1. Hypokalaemia
1. Pulmonary oedema
1. Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
1. Cerebral oedema

## Investigation

1. Venous blood
   - Urea and electrolytes: Hyponatremia, hyperkalaemia
   - Glucose: Hyperglycaemia
   - Bicarbonate: < 15 mmol/L
   - Acid-base status: pH < 7.3
1. Blood (or urine) analysis for ketones
1. ECG: To check acute myocardial infarction or electrolyte abnormalities
1. Chest X-ray: To check lung consolidation or pulmonary oedema
1. Infection screen
   - Full blood count [^2]
   - Blood and urine culture
   - CRP, Covid-19 swab
1. Pregnancy test: In all women of childbearing age

## Diagnosis

> 5% DKA can occur without hyperglycaemia (_euglycaemic DKA_ in type 1 diabetes in pregnancy and patients taking SGLT2 inhibitors)!

1. **Hyperglycaemia** (blood glucose > 11.1 mmol/L | 200 mg/dL) or **known diabetes**
1. **Hyperketonaemia** (≥ 3.0 mmol/L on fingerprick testing) or **ketonuria** (2+ on urine dipstick testing)
1. **Metabolic acidosis** (venous bicarbonate < 15 mmol/L and/or venous pH < 7.3 (H+ > 50 nmol/L))

- 1 mmol/L = 18 mg/dL
- pH 7.26 - 7.30 = mild DKA
- pH 7.0 - 7.25 = moderate DKA
- pH < 7.0 = severe DKA

## Management

> DKA is a medical emergency and treat in hospital!

### Emergency Management

<details>
<summary>Diagnostic criteria for diabetes</summary>

In asymptomatic patients, two diagnostic tests are required to confirm diabetes.

1. `Random blood glucose level or Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT)`[^3] ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL) **OR**
1. `Fasting blood glucose` ≥ 7 mmol/L (126 mg/dL) **OR**
1. `HbA1c` ≥ 48 mmol/mol (≥ 6.5%)

</details>

<details>
<summary>Indicators of severe DKA </summary>

1. Blood ketones > 6 mmol/L
1. Bicarbonate < 5 mmol/L
1. Venous/arterial pH < 7.0 (H+ > 100 nmol/L)

May also be:

1. Persistent hypotension or oliguria
1. Hypokalaemia (< 3.5 mmol/L)
1. GCS score < 12
1. O2 saturation < 92 % on air
1. Systolic BP < 90 mmHg
1. Heart rate > 100 or < 60 bpm
1. Anion gap > 16 mmol/L

</details>

1. Confirm diagnosis
1. Assess severity
1. `Fluid`
   - Add `10% glucose` 125 ml/hr when glucose < 14 mmol/L
   1. ~~SBP < 90 mmHg~~
      - Fluid bolus `Normal saline` 500 ml over 10-15 min
      - If SBP < 90 mmHg, Repeat
      - If SPB > 90 mmHg,
   1. _SBP > 90 mmHg_
      - 1 L `Normal saline` over 1 hr
      - Then, 1 L `Normal saline` over 2 hr
      - Then, 1 L `Normal saline` over 4 hr
      - Cautions if kidney or heart failure history present
      - Accurate fluid blanace and aim for urine output ≥ 0.5 ml/kg/hr
1. `Insulin`
   - Fixed Rate Insulin Infusion (FRII) 0.1 unit/kg/hr
   - Continue SC basal insulin analogue if normally taken
   - Assess response hourly
   - Is blood ketones falling ≥ 0.5 mmol/hr:
     - If _Yes_: Continue FRII until **DKA resolves**
     - If ~~No~~: Check infusion pump and adjust FRII
1. `Potassium`
   - Measure venous blood gas urgently; Reassess at 1 hr and 2 hourly thereafter
   - Maintain potassium 4-5 mmol/L
   - If _> 5.5 mmol/L_: No added KCL
   - If ~~< 5.5 mmol/L~~: Add 40 mmol/L KCL (premixed)

<details>
<summary>Resolution of DKA</summary>

1. Blood ketones ≤ 0.6 mmol/L AND
1. Venous pH > 7.3 AND
1. Bicarbonate > 15 mmol/L

</details>

### Ongoing Management

- After patient is biochemically stable, and can eat and drink, restore usual insulin regimen (SC injection)
- FRII should be continued for 30 min after short-acting insulin is given at a mealtime
- Review precipitating factors
  1.  Glycaemic control
  1.  Glucose monitoring
  1.  Insulin injection technique & injection sites [^4]

---

[^1]: Mild hypothermia: Acidosis → Vasodilation → Hypothermia (In infected patients, pyrexia may not be present initially)

[^2]: Leucocytosis occurs commonly in DKA, but generally represents a stress response rather than infection

[^3]: OGTT: 2 hrs after a 75 g glucose load

[^4]: Injecting into the same area too often can cause the skin to become lumpy or scarred, which can reduce insulin absorption. Other injection technique issues include withdrawing the needle too quickly, reusing a needle, or not mixing the insulin correctly.
