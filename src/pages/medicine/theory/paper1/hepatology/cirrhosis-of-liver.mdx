<details>
<summary>Causes of Jaundice</summary>

### Pre-hepatic

> Excessive breakdown of RBCs produces too many unconjugated bilirubin which overwhelm the liver even with normal liver function.

1. Hemolytic anemia (e.g., sickle cell disease, hereditary spherocytosis)
1. Infections (e.g., malaria, sepsis)
1. Ineffective erythropoiesis (e.g., thalassemia)
1. Blood transfusion reactions

### Hepatic

> Dysfunctional liver causes impairment in bilirubin conjugation and excretion.

1. Hepatocellular diseases (e.g., viral hepatitis, cirrhosis, alcoholic liver disease)
1. Metabolic disorders (e.g., Gilbert syndrome, Crigler-Najjar syndrome)
1. Drugs/toxins (e.g., paracetamol overdose, rifampin)

### Post-hepatic

> Bile flow is obstructed with normal liver function.

1. Gallstones (cholelithiasis)
1. Pancreatic cancer (tumor compressing bile duct)
1. Cholestasis (primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis)
1. Biliary atresia (in newborns)

| ---------------- | --------------- | --------------- | ------------ | ---------------- |
| **Pre-Hepatic**  | Normal          | Dark            | No           | **Unconjugated** |
| **Hepatic**      | Dark            | Pale            | Mild         | **Mixed (Both)** |
| **Post-Hepatic** | Dark            | Pale/Clay       | Severe       | **Conjugated**   |

## Investigation

1. **USG** or **CT** (Diagnosis test)
   - To detect cirrhosis, ascites, splenomegaly, portal vein dilatation
1. Blood tests
   - **Full blood count**: To detect anaemia
   - **Lipid profile**: For NAFLD
   - **Liver function tests, Liver enzymes**
   - **Viral hepatitis B, C**
   - **Serum iron, ferritin, total iron binding capacity**: For haemochromatosis
   - **Serum copper, urinary copper**: For Wilson's disease
   - **Random blood sugar**: For hypoglycemia
   - **Blood grouping & matching**: For blood transfusion
1. **OGD scopy**: For esophageal varices
1. **Ascites fluid analysis**: For spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
1. **Urinalysis, Creatinine**: For hepato-renal syndrome

## Management

### Supportive Management

- Daily calorie intake > 35 kcal/kg body weight
- Daily protein intake 1.2 - 1.5 g/kg
- A late evening snack to minimise overnight fasting
- Consider enteral feeding if oral feeding is inadequate
- Vitamin K, B1, Multivitamin, Glucose supplement

### Treatment of Underlying Cause

1. `Cessation of alcohol`: For **alcoholic cirrhosis**
1. `Anti-viral therapy`:
   - `Tenofovir`, `Entecavir`: For **hepatitis B**
   - `Sofosbuvir`, `Grazoprevir`: For **hepatitis C**
1. `Desferrioxamine`: For **haemochromatosis**
1. `D-penicillamine`: For **Wilson's disease**
1. Stop or avoid hepatotoxic drugs, nephrotoxic drugs and sedatives

### Treatment of Complications

- `Regular surveillance` for **hepatocellular carcinoma**
- `Bone mineral density` for **osteoporosis** and **fractures**
- `Terlipressin` + `Albumin infusion` for **hepato-renal syndrome**

<details>
<summary>Management of variceal bleeding in portal hypertension</summary>

#### Primary Prevention of Variceal Bleeding

- `Non-selective β-blocker` (`propranolol` or `nadolol`) **OR**
- `Non-cardioselective vasodiating β-blocker` (`carvedilol`)

#### Management of Acute Variceal Bleeding

<details>
<summary>Management of acute bleeding from oseophageal varices</summary>

1. Suspected variceal haemorrhage
1. Start IV `terlipressin` and `antibiotic` prophylaxis
1. Urgent upper GI Endoscopy
1. Varices present?
   - If ~~No~~: Stop `terlipressin` (treat as for non-variceal haemorrhage)
   - If _Yes_: Endoscopic therapy (variceal banding, sclerotherapy)→ Haemostasis?
     - If _Yes_: Continue `terlipressin` to 72 hrs; Introduce `β-blocker` as secondary prophylaxis; Enter patient into `endoscopic banding programme` to obliterate varices
     - If ~~No~~: Further endoscopic therapy or Balloon tamponade or Emergency TIPSS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Stent Shunt)

</details>

- Source of bleeding should always be confirmed by endoscopy
- Prophylactic `broad-spectrum antibiotics` (IV `cephalosporin` or `piperacillin/tazobactam`)

##### Pharmacological Management

- Vasoconstrictor `Terlipressin` until bleeding stops, then up to 72 hours
- Alternative: `Octreotide`

##### Non-Pharmacological Management

1. **Variceal ligation ('banding')** + Prophylactic `PPI` ± Endotracheal intubation
1. **Balloon tamponade** ± Endotracheal intubation
1. **TIPSS** (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Stent Shunt)
1. **Portosystemic shunt surgery**
1. **Oesophageal transection**

#### Secondary Prevention of Variceal Bleeding

- `β-blockers`
- Enter an `oesophageal banding programme` at 2-4-week intervals until the varices are obliterated

</details>

<details>
<summary>Management of hepatic encephalopathy</summary>

> Treat the underlying causes

1. `Lactulose` (15-30 mL)
   - Increase gradually until bowel movements twice daily
1. `Phosphate enemas`
   - In patients with refractory constipation or oral route is compromised
1. `Rifaximin` (550 mg twice daily)
   - Reduce recurent hepatic encephalopathy
1. `L-ornithine L-arginine` (LOLA)
   - To lower ammonia levels and improve encephalopathy
1. `Endotracheal intubation` if needed to protect airway
1. Dietary protein restriction is not recommended

</details>

<details>
<summary>Management of ascites</summary>

1. Restricting sodium intake
   - Low sodium diet
   - Drugs that cause sodium retention
1. Diuretics: `Spironolactone` + `Furosemide`
1. In refractory ascites:
   - `Paracentesis` + `IV colloid (human albumin solution)` for every 2-3 L of ascites drained
   - Alternative to paracentesis: `Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Stent Shunt` (TIPSS)

</details>

## Prognosis

<details>
<summary>Child-Pugh classification of prognosis in cirrhosis</summary>

> A BEAT

| Score                                              | 1     | 2       | 3      |
| :------------------------------------------------- | :---- | :------ | :----- |
| **`A`scites**                                      | None  | Mild    | Marked |
| **`B`ilirubin (mg/dL)**                            |       |         |        |
| Primary biliary cholangitis/sclerosing cholangitis | < 4   | 4-10    | > 10   |
| Other causes of cirrhosis                          | < 2   | 2-3     | > 3    |
| **`E`ncephalopathy**                               | None  | Mild    | Marked |
| **`A`lbumin** (g/dL)                               | > 3.5 | 2.8-3.5 | < 2.8  |
| **Prothrombin `t`ime**                             | < 4   | 4-6     | > 6    |

Add the individual scores:

- < 7 = Child's A (Well compensated)
- 7-9 = Child's B (Significant functional)
- \> 9 = Child's C (Decompensated)

</details>

- Prognosis is better when the underlying cause can be corrected
- Compensated patients have a relatively good prognosis
- Use `Child-Pugh's score` for prognosis

[^1]: **Dark urine** → Due to **increased conjugated bilirubin** (which is water-soluble and excreted in urine).

[^2]: **Pale stool** → Due to **lack of stercobilin** (formed from conjugated bilirubin in the intestine).

[^3]: **Pruritus** → Caused by **bile salt accumulation** in blood and skin, more common in post-hepatic jaundice.
