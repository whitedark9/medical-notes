# Pleural Effusion

> What are the causes of left-sided pleural effusion? How would you assess and investigate a
> case of a 40-year-old man presented with cough, fever for 2 weeks, and his CXR (PA) revealed
> moderate left-sided pleural effusion?

## Causes of Left-Sided Pleural Effusion

### 1. Parapneumonic & Infective Causes

- Bacterial pneumonia (esp. _Streptococcus pneumoniae_, _Staphylococcus aureus_).
- Tuberculous pleuritis.
- Lung abscess with pleural extension.
- Subphrenic abscess (left side).

### 2. Malignant Causes

- Primary lung cancer (left lung).
- Metastatic carcinoma (e.g., breast, ovary, stomach).
- Mesothelioma.
- Lymphoma.

### 3. Cardiovascular Causes

- Left ventricular failure (usually bilateral, but can be left-predominant).
- Post–myocardial infarction (Dressler’s syndrome).
- Pulmonary embolism with infarction (left lung).
- Pericarditis.

### 4. Abdominal & Subdiaphragmatic Causes

- Acute pancreatitis (_especially left-sided effusion due to pancreatic tail involvement_).
- Splenic abscess or rupture.
- Gastric carcinoma (transdiaphragmatic spread).

### 5. Trauma

- Left rib fractures with haemothorax.
- Penetrating injury to left chest.

### 6. Autoimmune & Inflammatory Causes

- Systemic lupus erythematosus.
- Rheumatoid arthritis (rarely unilateral but possible).
- Sarcoidosis.

---

## Assessment & Investigations

- Symptoms for 2 weeks → more likely **infective, inflammatory, or malignant** rather than acute traumatic or chronic systemic causes.

## I. Differential Diagnosis (DDx)

### 1. Infective Causes

- **Parapneumonic effusion** (secondary to bacterial pneumonia)
- **Tuberculous pleuritis** (common in subacute presentations)
- Lung abscess with pleural extension
- Subphrenic abscess (left side)

### 2. Malignant Causes

- Primary lung carcinoma (esp. adenocarcinoma)
- Metastatic carcinoma (breast, ovary, stomach)
- Lymphoma
- Mesothelioma

### 3. Abdominal Causes

- Acute pancreatitis (_esp. tail involvement → left effusion_)
- Splenic abscess / rupture

### 4. Others

- Pulmonary embolism with infarction
- Autoimmune (SLE, rheumatoid pleuritis)

## II. History

### A. Presenting Illness

- Onset, duration, and progression of **cough & fever**
- Nature of cough → productive (purulent → bacterial; blood-stained → TB/malignancy) or dry
- Chest pain → pleuritic type (infection), dull ache (malignancy)
- Breathlessness → degree & onset
- Fever pattern → continuous (pneumonia), low-grade evening rise (TB)

### B. Associated Symptoms

- Weight loss, night sweats (TB, malignancy)
- Haemoptysis (TB, malignancy)
- Orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea (cardiac)
- Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting (pancreatitis, subphrenic abscess)
- Joint pain, skin rash (autoimmune)

### C. Past & Risk Factors

- TB contact history
- Smoking, occupational dust/fibre exposure
- Past malignancy or family history
- Alcohol use (→ pancreatitis)
- Recent surgery or trauma

## III. Physical Examination

### A. General

- Fever, pallor, cachexia
- Lymphadenopathy (TB, lymphoma, metastatic cancer)
- Clubbing (TB, bronchiectasis, malignancy)
- Jaundice (malignancy, pancreatitis)

### B. Respiratory System

**Inspection:**

- Reduced chest movement on left side
- Bulging of intercostal spaces if large effusion
- Tracheal shift to opposite side (if large)

**Palpation:**

- Decreased chest expansion (left)
- Reduced tactile vocal fremitus (TVF) over effusion

**Percussion:**

- Stony dull note over effusion

**Auscultation:**

- Diminished or absent breath sounds over effusion
- Egophony just above fluid level

### C. Other Systems

- Abdominal exam → splenic enlargement, tenderness (pancreatitis, splenic abscess)
- Cardiac exam → murmurs, AF (embolic cause)
- Joint exam → synovitis (autoimmune)

## IV. Investigations

### A. For Diagnosis (Confirm Effusion & Characterize)

1. **Chest X-ray (PA & lateral decubitus)** → confirms & estimates volume, shifts mediastinum
2. **Ultrasound chest** → detects small volumes, guides aspiration
3. **Diagnostic thoracocentesis**:

   - Gross appearance (clear, turbid, bloody, chylous)
   - **Pleural fluid analysis**:

     - Protein, LDH → **Light’s criteria** (exudate vs transudate)
     - Glucose, pH, cell count & differential
     - Gram stain, AFB smear, bacterial/fungal cultures
     - Cytology (malignancy)

### B. For Causes

- **CBC & ESR/CRP** (infection, inflammation, malignancy)
- **Sputum AFB, culture** (TB, bacterial)
- **Mantoux / IGRA** (TB)
- **CT thorax** (parenchymal lung disease, malignancy, lymph nodes)
- **Serum amylase/lipase** (pancreatitis)
- **ANA, RF** (autoimmune)
- **Blood cultures** (empyema, sepsis)

### C. For Complications

- ABG (hypoxia)
- Ultrasound abdomen (subphrenic abscess, splenic pathology)
