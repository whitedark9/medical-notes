# Anaphylaxis

> Severe, potentially life-threatening **systemic hypersensitivity reaction** → rapid onset → due to sudden **mast cell and basophil degranulation** with massive mediator release (mainly histamine, tryptase, leukotrienes).

- Most commonly **IgE-mediated** (Type I hypersensitivity) but can also be **non-IgE mediated**.

## Pathophysiology

**Trigger** (allergen: food, drug, insect venom, latex)
→ **IgE antibodies** bound to mast cells/basophils cross-link
→ **Degranulation** → release of histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, leukotrienes
→ **Vasodilation** + ↑ vascular permeability → **hypotension, shock**
→ **Bronchospasm** + mucosal oedema → **airway obstruction**
→ **Skin and GI symptoms** due to smooth muscle contraction and capillary leakage.

## Common Causes

1. **Foods**: peanuts, shellfish, milk, eggs
2. **Drugs**: penicillin, NSAIDs, anaesthetic agents
3. **Insect stings**: bees, wasps
4. **Latex**
5. **Idiopathic** (10–20%)

## Clinical Features (Rapid Onset: minutes to \<2 hrs)

- **Airway**: hoarseness, stridor, tongue swelling
- **Breathing**: wheeze, bronchospasm, hypoxia
- **Circulation**: hypotension, tachycardia, collapse
- **Skin/Mucosa**: urticaria, flushing, angioedema
- **GI**: abdominal cramps, vomiting, diarrhoea

## Emergency Management (ABCDEF approach)

1.  **Airway**

    - Assess patency, prepare for advanced airway if swelling/stridor

2.  **Breathing**

    - High-flow O₂ (15 L/min via non-rebreather)
    - Nebulised salbutamol if bronchospasm persists after adrenaline

3.  **Circulation**

    - Lay patient **flat with legs elevated** unless respiratory distress (then sit up)
    - `IM Adrenaline` — **first-line, do not delay**

    - Adult: **0.5 mg** (0.5 mL of 1:1000) IM into mid-anterolateral thigh
    - Repeat every 5 min if no improvement

    - Establish IV access, give rapid crystalloid bolus (20 mL/kg) if hypotensive

4.  **Disability**

    - Monitor consciousness (hypoxia, shock can cause altered sensorium)

5.  **Exposure**

    - Look for urticaria, angioedema, stings, injection site

6.  **Further measures**

    - **Adjunct drugs** (AFTER adrenaline):

      - `Chlorphenamine` (10 mg IV/IM) — H₁ antihistamine
      - `Hydrocortisone` (200 mg IV) — may reduce biphasic reaction risk

      - Monitor ECG, SpO₂, BP continuously
      - **Observe for ≥ 6 hrs** (biphasic reactions can occur)

## Post-Emergency Management

- Identify trigger → allergy testing later
- Educate on avoidance
- Prescribe **adrenaline auto-injector** (EpiPen)
- Provide written **anaphylaxis action plan**
