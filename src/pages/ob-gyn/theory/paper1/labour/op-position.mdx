# Occipito-Posterior (OP) Position

## Causes of High Head at Term (Unengaged Head)

→ Normally, fetal head should be **engaged by 36 weeks** in primigravida

### A. Maternal Causes

1. **Cephalopelvic Disproportion (CPD)**
   → Pelvis too small or fetal head too large
2. **Uterine abnormalities**
   → Fibroids, bicornuate uterus, uterine septum
3. **Pelvic tumors**
   → Cervical fibroids or ovarian masses obstructing inlet
4. **High parity**
   → Lax uterine muscles, poor tone → abnormal lie

### B. Fetal Causes

1. **Occipito-posterior (OP) position**
   → Head poorly flexed → larger diameter (occipitofrontal 11.5 cm) presents
2. **Malpresentations**
   → Brow, face, or deflexed vertex → cannot engage
3. **Hydrocephalus**
   → Enlarged head can't descend
4. **Macrosomia**
   → Head too large
5. **Prematurity**
   → Head too small to engage

## Outcomes

### A. Labour Outcomes

| Outcome                             | Mechanism                                             |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Prolonged labor**                 | Poor flexion → larger diameters → slower descent      |
| **Deep transverse arrest**          | Head fails to rotate anteriorly → stuck in mid-pelvis |
| **Increased instrumental delivery** | Vacuum or forceps required for rotation or delivery   |
| **Higher cesarean section rate**    | Failed rotation or arrest                             |
| **Back pain**                       | Persistent pressure from occiput on maternal sacrum   |
| **Premature rupture of membranes**  | Due to high, poorly fitting head                      |
| **Maternal exhaustion**             | Due to prolonged, painful labor                       |

### B. Neonatal Outcomes

| Outcome               | Mechanism                                   |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Birth trauma**      | Instrumental delivery, difficult extraction |
| **Asphyxia**          | Prolonged second stage or cord compression  |
| **Caput & moulding**  | Due to prolonged engagement at brim         |
| **Neonatal jaundice** | From trauma and bruising                    |

## Diagnosis

### A. Antenatal Suspicion (Late Pregnancy)

- **Fetal back** → felt in **flanks**, limbs anteriorly
- **Head high and deflexed** → may be mobile at term
- **Leopold's maneuver**: Back difficult to palpate midline

### B. In Labour (Confirmed Diagnosis)

| Method                  | Findings |
| ----------------------- | -------- |
| **Vaginal Examination** |          |

- Fontanelles: **Anterior fontanelle (diamond-shaped)** more accessible
- **Posterior fontanelle** harder to feel or located **posteriorly**
- **Sutures**: Sagittal suture in **oblique** or **transverse** diameter
  \| **Digital location of occiput** | Occiput felt in **posterior quadrants** (right or left sacroiliac regions)
  \| **Caput & Moulding** | Common due to prolonged labor |

### C. Ultrasound (if unclear)

- Confirms position of occiput, head attitude, and engagement

## Complications During Labour

> Due to malrotation, deflexion, and larger presenting diameter

### Maternal Complications

| Complication                            | Pathophysiology (How it develops)                                                                                          |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Prolonged labour**                    | Deflexed head → larger occipitofrontal diameter (11.5 cm) presents → slow descent & rotation → delay in 1st and 2nd stages |
| **Deep transverse arrest**              | Failure of internal rotation → occiput remains posterior or in transverse → head stuck in mid-pelvis                       |
| **Increased instrumental delivery**     | Non-rotation or delay → vacuum/forceps needed for rotation and delivery                                                    |
| **Cesarean section**                    | If failed instrumental attempt, fetal distress, or arrest in 2nd stage                                                     |
| **Backache**                            | Due to fetal occiput pressing on maternal sacrum throughout labour                                                         |
| **Perineal trauma**                     | Larger diameter, rapid delivery or instrumental use → ↑ risk of tears                                                      |
| **Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)**         | Prolonged labour → uterine atony → bleeding in 3rd stage                                                                   |
| **Uterine rupture** _(rare)_            | If CPD or deep arrest persists with strong contractions, especially in multiparas                                          |
| **Exhaustion and psychological trauma** | Long, painful labour + failed delivery attempts                                                                            |

### Fetal Complications

| Complication                | Pathophysiology                                                   |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Birth asphyxia**          | Prolonged labour or cord compression → ↓ placental perfusion      |
| **Intracranial hemorrhage** | Especially with instrumental rotation → trauma to fetal skull     |
| **Caput succedaneum**       | Prolonged pressure of head against cervix → scalp edema           |
| **Excessive moulding**      | Bones of fetal skull overlap due to prolonged labour and pressure |
| **Facial or skull trauma**  | Forceps or vacuum traction in OP → bruising, facial nerve injury  |
| **Neonatal jaundice**       | Bruising → increased bilirubin load postnatally                   |

## Management

### I. Antenatal Period

- **Most OP positions spontaneously rotate to OA before labour**
- No active management if:

  - Singleton
  - No anomaly
  - Head not engaged but mobile

#### Counseling:

- Inform about possible **prolonged labour**
- Delivery at **facility with CS capability**
- **Pelvimetry** not routinely required

---

### II. Intrapartum Management

#### 1st Stage of Labour

##### Assessment

- **CTG monitoring**
- Confirm position by:

  - **Vaginal exam** → sagittal suture in oblique or transverse plane, anterior fontanelle palpable
  - **Ultrasound** (if needed) → occiput posterior

##### Management Plan

- **Expectant**: 90% rotate to OA during labour
- **Posture changes**:

  - Encourage **hands-and-knees**, side-lying, pelvic rocking
  - May help spontaneous rotation

- **Analgesia**:

  - **Epidural** relieves severe back pain
  - May prolong 2nd stage slightly but improves cooperation

##### Monitoring:

- **Progress** with partograph
- Assess descent, flexion, rotation
- Check for **caput/moulding**

#### 2nd Stage of Labour

> Management depends on **progress**, **rotation**, **descent**, and **maternal/fetal condition**

#### A. If Spontaneous Rotation to OA →

- Continue with **normal vaginal delivery**

#### B. Persistent OP with Good Progress

(→ Occiput posterior, but descent continues)

- Allow **spontaneous delivery** if:

  - Good contractions
  - Maternal effort effective
  - No fetal distress
  - Experienced birth attendant present

> **Spontaneous vaginal delivery in OP is possible in \~30%**.

#### C. Arrest in 2nd Stage / Failure to Rotate or Descend

Options:

1. **Manual rotation + forceps**
2. **Rotational forceps** (e.g., Kielland)
3. **Vacuum rotation**
4. **Cesarean section**

##### 1. Manual Rotation + Forceps Delivery

- Rotate fetal head to OA using internal hand
- Apply forceps after rotation
- Requires:

  - Adequate pelvis
  - Full dilation
  - Skilled operator

##### 2. Rotational Forceps (Kielland)

- Rotates fetal head to OA and delivers
- Higher risk of:

  - Vaginal tears
  - Fetal trauma

- Use only by **experienced obstetrician**

##### 3. Vacuum-Assisted Rotation

- Can rotate and deliver OP if:

  - Head is low
  - No CPD
  - Good fetal condition

- Apply cup at **flexion point** (just anterior to posterior fontanelle)

##### 4. Cesarean Section

- Indications:

  - Deep transverse arrest
  - Arrest of descent
  - Failed instrumental delivery
  - Fetal distress
  - CPD

- Safer option in **primigravida with unrotated OP**

#### 3rd Stage of Labour

- Active management:

  - **Oxytocin 10 IU IM**
  - Controlled cord traction
  - Uterine massage

- Watch for **PPH** (due to prolonged labour/uterine atony)

---

### III. Postpartum Care

#### Mother

- Monitor for:

  - **Perineal trauma**
  - **PPH**
  - **Anemia**, infection if traumatic delivery

- Emotional support (long, difficult labour)

#### Baby

- Check for:

  - **Bruising, caput**
  - **Asphyxia** if prolonged 2nd stage
  - **Instrumental trauma**

---

### IV. Summary Management by Labour Stage

| Stage                                              | Management                                    |
| -------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Antenatal**                                      | Expectant; monitor fetal lie                  |
| **1st Stage**                                      | Monitor rotation, pain relief, posture change |
| **2nd Stage**                                      | → If rotates: normal delivery                 |
| → If persistent OP with descent: allow spontaneous |                                               |
| → If arrest: manual/forceps/vacuum/CS              |                                               |
| **3rd Stage**                                      | AMTSL, watch for PPH                          |
| **Postpartum**                                     | Monitor trauma, neonatal wellbeing            |
