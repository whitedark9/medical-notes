# Induction of Labour (IOL)

> **Artificial initiation of uterine contractions** before spontaneous labor onset with the goal of achieving **vaginal delivery**

## Indications for Induction of Labour

### A. Maternal Indications

1. **Post-dated pregnancy**
   → >40 weeks + 10 days (i.e., >41+3 weeks)
2. **Hypertensive disorders**
   → Pre-eclampsia, gestational hypertension
3. **Diabetes mellitus**
   → Especially with macrosomia or poor control
4. **PROM/PPROM**
   → To reduce risk of infection
5. **Maternal medical diseases**
   → Cardiac disease, renal disease, cholestasis
6. **Antepartum hemorrhage (stable placenta previa excluded)**
   → Only if bleeding controlled and fetus mature

### B. Fetal Indications

1. **IUGR** with reassuring fetal status
2. **Fetal death (IUFD)**
3. **Oligohydramnios**
4. **Rh isoimmunization (non-severe)**
5. **Congenital anomalies incompatible with life**

## Timing of Induction

| Indication                   | Recommended Timing                            |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Post-term**                | 41+0 to 42+0 weeks                            |
| **PROM at term**             | Within 24 hrs                                 |
| **Pre-eclampsia**            | 37 weeks (earlier if severe)                  |
| **GDM (well controlled)**    | 39–40 weeks                                   |
| **IUGR**                     | 37–38 weeks (if fetal monitoring normal)      |
| **IUFD**                     | At diagnosis                                  |
| **Oligohydramnios**          | 37–38 weeks                                   |
| **Cholestasis of pregnancy** | 37–38 weeks (↑ risk of stillbirth after that) |

## Why Choose Induction of Labour? (Reasons)

| Reason                                      | Explanation                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Prevent stillbirth**                      | Especially in post-term, IUGR, cholestasis, IUFD                                                      |
| **Avoid maternal complications**            | In preeclampsia, diabetes, renal disease                                                              |
| **Limit infection risk**                    | In PROM/PPROM                                                                                         |
| **Elective reasons** (rare, selected cases) | E.g., maternal anxiety, distance from hospital, previous rapid labor – but must be justified and safe |
| **Fetal compromise**                        | Better neonatal outcome if delivered in time                                                          |

## Important Considerations Before Induction

- **Confirm gestational age**
- **Assess Bishop score** (cervical favourability)
- **Rule out contraindications** (see below)
- **Ensure availability of emergency CS**

## Contraindications to IOL

- Previous **classical cesarean section** or uterine rupture
- **Placenta previa**
- **Malpresentation** (e.g. transverse lie)
- **Cephalopelvic disproportion**
- **Active genital herpes**
- **Fetal distress requiring immediate CS**

## Methods of Induction

### A. Medical Methods

#### 1. Prostaglandins (PGE2 / Misoprostol)

- **Route**: Vaginal (gel, pessary), Oral, or Sublingual
- **Example**:
  - `Dinoprostone (PGE2)` – 0.5 mg gel every 6 hrs (max 3 doses)
  - `Misoprostol (PGE1 analogue)` – 25 mcg orally or vaginally every 4 hrs

| Advantages                          | Disadvantages                            | Risks                                    |
| ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Effective for **cervical ripening** | May cause uterine hyperstimulation       | **Hyperstimulation**, fetal distress     |
| Easy administration                 | Not suitable after prior uterine surgery | Uterine rupture (esp. if scarred uterus) |
| Useful in both term and IUFD cases  | Requires monitoring                      | GI side effects (nausea, vomiting)       |

#### 2. Oxytocin Infusion

> **Route**: IV drip (e.g., 2 mU/min increasing every 30 mins up to 20 mU/min).

| Advantages                      | Disadvantages                   | Risks                                        |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Useful when cervix already ripe | Ineffective with unripe cervix  | **Hypertonic contractions**, fetal hypoxia   |
| Controlled via pump             | Requires hospital setup and CTG | Uterine rupture (esp. grand multipara)       |
| Can be titrated                 | Continuous monitoring required  | Water intoxication (rare) with prolonged use |

### B. Mechanical Methods

#### 3. Foley Catheter (Transcervical balloon)

> 16–18F Foley inserted into cervix → inflated with 30–60 mL saline → gentle pressure ripens cervix.

| Advantages                     | Disadvantages                       | Risks                                 |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Safe in **scarred uterus**     | Slower effect than prostaglandins   | Discomfort, bleeding                  |
| Lower risk of hyperstimulation | Requires trained personnel          | Infection (rare)                      |
| Inexpensive, widely available  | Not effective in high closed cervix | Premature rupture of membranes (rare) |

### C. Surgical Method

#### 4. Artificial Rupture of Membranes (ARM / Amniotomy)

> Using **Amnihook** to rupture membranes

| Advantages                           | Disadvantages                        | Risks                                                        |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Quick & natural labour often follows | Only if cervix is **ripe & dilated** | **Cord prolapse** if head not engaged                        |
| Enhances effect of oxytocin          | May fail if unfavourable cervix      | Chorioamnionitis if prolonged labour                         |
| Diagnostic for liquor status         | Not suitable in HIV or GBS+ cases    | ↑ Risk of operative delivery if not followed by contractions |

### D. Other Methods (Less Common / Not Recommended)

| Method                         | Comments                                                                |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Membrane sweeping**          | Gentle digital separation of membranes → may reduce need for formal IOL |
| **Castor oil / herbal agents** | Not evidence-based, may cause GI side effects                           |
| **Nipple stimulation**         | Oxytocin release → mild uterine contractions, but not reliable          |

## **Summary Table: Methods, Uses, Pros & Cons**

| Method                                | Best Use                          | Pros                  | Cons / Risks                             |
| ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Prostaglandins (PGE2/Misoprostol)** | Unripe cervix                     | Effective ripening    | Hyperstimulation, not for scarred uterus |
| **Oxytocin**                          | Ripe cervix or after ARM          | Titrable, predictable | Needs CTG, water intoxication risk       |
| **Foley catheter**                    | Scarred uterus, limited resources | Safe, low risk        | Discomfort, slower onset                 |
| **ARM**                               | Dilated, engaged head             | Immediate effect      | Cord prolapse, infection                 |
