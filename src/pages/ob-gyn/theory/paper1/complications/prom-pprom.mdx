# PROM / PPROM

> **PROM (Premature Rupture of Membranes)** is the rupture of fetal membranes **after 37 weeks** gestation but **before onset of labor**.

> **PPROM (Preterm PROM)** is the rupture of membranes **before 37 weeks** gestation and **before onset of labor**.

## Differential Diagnoses

1. Urinary incontinence (Stress or urge)
2. Physiological vaginal discharge (increased in pregnancy)
3. Vaginitis / Cervicitis (bacterial vaginosis, candidiasis)
4. Show (blood-tinged mucus)
5. Seminal fluid (after intercourse)
6. Vesicovaginal fistula (rare)

## Diagnosis

### 1. History

- Sudden painless **gush of fluid**
- Constant **wetness of undergarments**
- May have decreased fetal movements or contractions

### 2. Examination

#### Per Speculum Exam (Sterile)

- Pooling of fluid in posterior fornix
- Direct leakage of clear fluid from cervical os
- **NO digital vaginal exam** unless in labor (↑ risk of infection)

### 3. Investigations

| Test                           | Description                                                           |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Nitrazine test**             | Vaginal pH >7 → turns **blue** (amniotic fluid is alkaline)           |
| **Ferning test**               | Dry sample of fluid → microscope shows **fern-like crystals**         |
| **Amnisure / Actim PROM**      | Detects placental proteins (e.g., PAMG-1, IGFBP-1) → high sensitivity |
| **Ultrasound**                 | ↓ Amniotic fluid index (AFI) supports diagnosis                       |
| **Ultrasound fetal wellbeing** | FHR, growth, movements                                                |

## Complications of PROM / PPROM

### A. Maternal Complications

| Type          | Complication                                 |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Infective** | Chorioamnionitis, endometritis, sepsis       |
| **Obstetric** | Placental abruption, PPH, operative delivery |
| **Other**     | Anxiety, prolonged hospitalization           |

### B. Fetal Complications

#### Term PROM

- Cord prolapse
- Ascending infection
- Neonatal sepsis

#### Preterm PROM

- Prematurity
- RDS, IVH, NEC
- Oligohydramnios
- pulmonary hypoplasia
- Cord prolapse
- Fetal deformities (if early rupture)
- Growth restriction

## Management

### Initial Approach: ABCDEF (Emergency Management)

> If signs of **maternal sepsis**, fetal distress, or **cord prolapse**, manage immediately

- **A – Airway** → Ensure airway if unstable (usually not an issue)
- **B – Breathing** → Check vitals, oxygen if needed
- **C – Circulation** → IV access, fluids, monitor BP, pulse
- **D – Disability** → Consciousness, pain, uterine tenderness
- **E – Exposure** → Temperature, per speculum (not digital), signs of infection
- **F – Fetus** → CTG (if viable), ultrasound (growth, AFI, presentation)

### Detailed Management Plan by Gestational Age

#### I. General Measures (All Cases)

1. Admission to hospital
2. Confirm diagnosis (Pooling, Nitrazine, Ferning, Amnisure)
3. Sterile speculum exam – NO digital exam unless labor suspected
4. Investigations:

   - CBC, CRP, high vaginal swab (HVS)
   - Blood group and crossmatch
   - Urinalysis and culture
   - CTG if \>26–28 weeks
   - Ultrasound (AFI, lie, presentation, growth, anomalies)

#### II. Management by Gestational Age

#### A. \<24 Weeks (Previable)

- **Expectant management with counselling**
- High risk of:

  - Pulmonary hypoplasia
  - Limb deformities
  - Infection

- **Counsel parents** → poor prognosis
- **Consider termination** if infection, bleeding, or fetal death

#### B. 24–34 Weeks (Main Window for Intervention)

> Most important phase for conservative + prophylactic management.

##### 1. Antibiotics (to prolong latency & reduce infection)

- `Erythromycin` 250 mg QID × 10 days
  → Avoid `amoxicillin`-`clavulanate` (↑ NEC risk)

##### 2. Corticosteroids

- `Betamethasone` 12 mg IM × 2 doses, 24 hrs apart
  _(or `Dexamethasone` 6 mg IM q12h × 4 doses)_
  → Reduces RDS, IVH, NEC

##### 3. Tocolytics (Controversial)

- Generally **not recommended** in PPROM
  → May consider **48 hrs max** to complete steroids if no infection or labor

##### 4. Magnesium Sulfate

- If **\<32 weeks**, give for **neuroprotection**

  - 4 g IV bolus over 20 min
  - Then 1 g/hour for 24 hrs or until delivery
    → Discontinue if delivery occurs

##### 5. Monitoring

- Daily maternal temp, pulse, CRP, WBC
- Monitor for **chorioamnionitis**:

  - Fever
  - Uterine tenderness
  - Foul-smelling discharge
  - Maternal/fetal tachycardia

##### 6. Fetal Monitoring

- Daily CTG (if viable)
- Weekly ultrasound for growth, AFI
- Dopplers if IUGR suspected

##### 7. Delivery Indications

- Clinical chorioamnionitis
- Fetal distress
- Severe IUGR or placental insufficiency
- Completed 34 weeks (elective delivery)

#### C. 34–37 Weeks

> Balance between risk of infection vs benefits of maturity

- Most centers **offer delivery** after **34 weeks**

  - Induce labor if cephalic, favorable cervix
  - Caesarean if obstetric indication

- Antibiotics: Short course if latency desired (controversial)
- **Steroids**: Give if not previously administered and \<35 weeks

#### D. >37 Weeks (Term PROM)

→ **Induce labor** (within 24 hrs)

- Give **GBS prophylaxis** if indicated
- Observe for infection

### III. Contraindications to Expectant Management

- Clinical chorioamnionitis
- Severe bleeding or abruption
- Fetal distress or anomaly incompatible with life
- Labor or advanced cervical dilatation
- Cord prolapse
- IUFD

### IV. Postpartum Care

#### Maternal

- Monitor for endometritis, sepsis
- Watch for retained placenta, PPH

#### Neonatal

- NICU support if \<37 weeks
- Watch for:

  - RDS
  - Sepsis
  - NEC
  - Jaundice

## Summary Table

| Gestation       | Management                                                 |
| --------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **\<24 weeks**  | Expectant, poor prognosis, counsel parents                 |
