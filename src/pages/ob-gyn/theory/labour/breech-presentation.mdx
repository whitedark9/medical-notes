# Breech Presentation

> Breech presentation = Longitudinal lie with fetal buttocks or lower extremities presenting first at the pelvic brim.

- Incidence: \~3–4% of all term pregnancies.

## Types of Breech Presentation

1. **Frank breech** (65%) – Hips flexed, knees extended
2. **Complete breech** – Hips and knees flexed
3. **Footling breech** – One/both hips and knees extended (incomplete)
4. **Kneeling breech** – Rare; hips extended, knees flexed

## Causes of Breech Presentation

### (Important for both diagnosis & failure of correction)

1. **Prematurity** (most common)
2. **Uterine anomalies** (e.g., bicornuate, septate uterus)
3. **Multiple pregnancy**
4. **Placenta previa**
5. **Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios**
6. **Fetal anomalies** (e.g., hydrocephalus, anencephaly)
7. **Uterine fibroids or tumors**
8. **Previous breech delivery**

## Diagnosis

### Clinical:

- Abdominal palpation: Head felt in fundus, soft breech in pelvis
- Vaginal exam: Soft mass (buttocks) instead of hard head

### Investigation for confirmation:

- **Ultrasound** (main): confirms presentation, fetal weight, attitude of head, placental location, amniotic fluid, anomalies
- **X-ray** (rarely): if needed for skeletal abnormalities
- **MRI**: for selected cases (e.g., suspected pelvic abnormality)

## Management of Breech Presentation

### Pre-conception (if recurrent)

- Treat correctable causes (e.g., uterine anomalies, fibroids)
- Pre-conception counselling for women with previous breech

### Antenatal Care

1. **Confirm diagnosis by 36 weeks**
2. **Assess suitability for external cephalic version (ECV)**

### External Cephalic Version (ECV)

- **Timing**: 36 weeks (primigravida), 37 weeks (multipara)
- **Procedure**: Manipulation under ultrasound guidance
- **Before ECV**:

  - Tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline) may be given
  - CTG monitoring before and after

- **Contraindications**:

  1. Placenta previa
  2. Multiple pregnancy
  3. Oligohydramnios
  4. Uterine anomaly
  5. Fetal distress or abnormal CTG

- **Risks**:

  - Placental abruption
  - Umbilical cord accidents
  - Preterm labor

- **Causes of Failure**:

  1. Uterine tone or anomaly
  2. Oligohydramnios
  3. Anterior placenta
  4. Engaged breech
  5. Fetal anomalies

### Intrapartum Management

#### 1. **Timing of Delivery**

- **Elective Caesarean**: 39 weeks (recommended in many cases)
- **Spontaneous labour**: If planned vaginal breech delivery, admit early for monitoring

#### 2. **Mode of Delivery**

##### A. Caesarean Section (recommended in many cases)

- **Advantages**:

  - Lower perinatal morbidity/mortality
  - Less risk of birth trauma

- **Risks**:

  - Surgical complications
  - Future uterine rupture risk

##### B. Vaginal Breech Delivery

- **Prerequisites (for safe vaginal delivery)**:

  1. Frank or complete breech
  2. Gestational age ≥37 weeks
  3. Estimated fetal weight 2.5–3.5 kg
  4. No fetal anomalies
  5. Normal pelvis
  6. Continuous fetal monitoring available
  7. Skilled obstetrician present
  8. Informed consent

- **Steps of Delivery**:

  - Allow spontaneous delivery till umbilicus
  - Use **Pinard’s maneuver** if leg does not deliver
  - **Lovset’s maneuver** to deliver arms
  - **Delivery of aftercoming head** using:

    1. **Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit maneuver**
    2. **Forceps (Piper’s)**
    3. **Burns-Marshall technique**

## Complications of Vaginal Breech Delivery

### Fetal:

- Cord prolapse
- Birth trauma: fracture, brachial plexus injury
- Asphyxia from delayed after-coming head
- Intracranial hemorrhage
- Fetal death

### Maternal:

- Genital tract laceration
- Uterine rupture (rare)
- Prolonged labor
- Psychological trauma

## Postpartum

- **Monitor baby**: For trauma, hypoxia
- **Evaluate mother**: For genital tract injury
- **Counsel for future pregnancies**:

  - Recurrence risk \~10%
  - Offer early scan in next pregnancy
  - Consider elective CS if breech persists

## Summary of Management Options by Patient

### Case 1: 30yo G2P1+0 at 37w in labor, breech, no complications

- **Option**: Trial of vaginal breech delivery (meets criteria)
- **Why**: Multipara, EFW 3 kg, term, no complications
- **If complications**: Proceed to emergency CS

### Case 2: 28yo primigravida at 38w, suspected breech

- **Investigations**: Ultrasound
- **Causes**: Primigravidity, uterine anomaly, placenta previa, fetal anomaly, polyhydramnios
- **Dangers**: Cord prolapse, head entrapment, trauma
- **Procedure**: Offer ECV
- **Causes of ECV failure**: Oligohydramnios, uterine tone, engaged breech, fetal anomaly

### Case 3: 25yo G2 at 37w, extended breech confirmed

- **Options**:

  - ECV if suitable
  - Elective CS
  - Planned vaginal breech if meets criteria

- **Advantages**:

  - CS: lower fetal risk
  - Vaginal: avoid surgery

- **Risks**:

  - CS: maternal surgical risks
  - Vaginal: higher fetal risks, especially if primigravida or footling
