# Puerperal Pyrexia & Puerperal Sepsis

## Puerperal Pyrexia

- **Any fever ≥38°C (100.4°F)**
- Occurring **within the first 14 days** postpartum
- On **any 2 consecutive days** (excluding the first 24 hours)

## Puerperal Sepsis[^1]

- **Infection of the genital tract** occurring **at any time between onset of labor and the 42nd day postpartum**, accompanied by:

  - Fever ≥38°C
  - Pelvic pain
  - Abnormal vaginal discharge/foul-smelling lochia
  - Delay in uterine involution

[^1]: Sepsis = systemic inflammatory response to infection

## Sources of Infection in Puerperal Sepsis

| Source                              | Explanation                                                          |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Endometrium (endometritis)**      | Most common source → infected placental bed                          |
| **Vaginal/cervical lacerations**    | Open wounds → easy bacterial entry                                   |
| **Episiotomy or perineal tears**    | Poor hygiene or suture technique can lead to infection               |
| **Cesarean section wound**          | Surgical site infection common in emergency or unsterile settings    |
| **Retained products of conception** | Necrotic tissue acts as nidus for infection                          |
| **Ascending vaginal flora**         | Opportunistic organisms ascend post-delivery when cervix is open     |
| **Invasive procedures**             | Multiple vaginal exams, unsterile instruments → direct contamination |
| **Urinary tract infection**         | Catheterization can introduce bacteria → ascending UTI               |
| **Breast infections (mastitis)**    | Can cause fever in postpartum but not genital source                 |

## Common Causative Organisms

### A. Endogenous (from vaginal flora)

1. **Group A Streptococcus (GAS)** – _Streptococcus pyogenes_
   → Rapidly invasive, can cause **toxic shock**

2. **Group B Streptococcus (GBS)** – _Streptococcus agalactiae_
   → Neonatal and maternal sepsis

3. _Escherichia coli_
   → From GI tract, causes UTI, wound infection

4. **Anaerobes** – _Bacteroides spp._, _Peptostreptococcus_
   → Associated with **foul-smelling lochia**

5. _Enterococcus spp._

6. _Staphylococcus aureus_
   → Especially in wound infections

### B. Exogenous (Hospital-acquired)

- _Pseudomonas_
- _Klebsiella_
- _Proteus_
- _MRSA_ (rare but serious)

## Management

### Emergency Measure

#### A. Airway

- Ensure airway is patent
- Oxygen if saturation \<94%

#### B. Breathing

- Monitor RR, SpO₂
- Provide supplemental oxygen (mask/nasal cannula) if hypoxic

#### C. Circulation

- **2 large-bore IV lines**
- Start **fluid resuscitation** (e.g. NS or RL bolus 1–2 L)
- Monitor pulse, BP, capillary refill
- Insert **urinary catheter** → strict input/output charting
- **Blood culture** before antibiotics
- CBC, CRP, LFT, RFT, coagulation profile, blood group/cross-match
- **Lactate level** → to assess sepsis severity
- Consider **ICU** if shock or organ failure

#### D. Definitive Treatment

##### 1. Broad-Spectrum IV Antibiotics (within 1 hour)

→ _Start empiric therapy, then modify based on culture_

| Antibiotic Regimen                       | Dose & Route                                 |
| ---------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| **IV Clindamycin** + **Gentamicin**      | Clinda 900 mg IV 8hrly + Gent 5 mg/kg/day IV |
| ± **Ampicillin**                         | 2 g IV 6 hourly if Enterococcus suspected    |
| Alternative: **Piperacillin-tazobactam** | 4.5 g IV 8hrly                               |
| If septic shock: **Meropenem**           | 1 g IV 8hrly                                 |

> Duration: Minimum **7–10 days** or until afebrile for 48–72 hrs

##### 2. Source Control

- **Evacuation of retained products of conception (RPOC)**
  → Suspected with persistent bleeding, subinvolution, USG findings
  → Do **gentle suction curettage** only after stabilizing patient
- **Drain abscess** (if pelvic abscess, infected episiotomy)
  → USG-guided or surgical drainage
- **Wound care** for infected CS or episiotomy site

#### E. Evaluation for Complications

- Monitor for:

  - **Endometritis**
  - **Pelvic abscess**
  - **Septic pelvic thrombophlebitis**
  - **Peritonitis**
  - **Septic shock**
  - **Multi-organ dysfunction**

- **Imaging**:

  - **Pelvic USG** → RPOC or abscess
  - **CT/MRI** → Deep pelvic infection or thrombosis

#### F. Follow-up and Supportive Care

- Analgesia and antipyretics (e.g., paracetamol)
- Maintain hydration and nutrition
- Thromboprophylaxis (LMWH) if immobile
- Encourage breastfeeding (if no contraindications)
- Psychosocial support

### Additional Considerations

| Situation                          | Management                                                 |
| ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Breast infection/mastitis**      | Dicloxacillin/Cloxacillin + continued breastfeeding        |
| **Severe sepsis/septic shock**     | ICU care, vasopressors (e.g., norepinephrine), ventilation |
| **Anaemia due to sepsis/bleeding** | Transfuse packed RBCs if Hb \<7 or symptomatic             |

### When to Perform Hysterectomy (Life-Saving)

→ If unresponsive to antibiotics + surgical debridement
→ **Indications**:

- Uterine necrosis
- Uncontrolled hemorrhage
- Clostridial myometritis
- Uterine rupture with infection

### Contraception Post-Sepsis

- Delay IUD insertion until complete recovery
- Safe options: **Progestin-only pills, injectables**, condoms
- Avoid estrogen-containing pills in septic thrombophlebitis
