# Gangrene

> Gangrene is `necrosis of tissue` due to `ischemia` or `infection`.

## Types

1. `Dry gangrene`: Ischemic necrosis due to arterial occlusion without infection.
1. `Wet gangrene`: Superimposed bacterial infection on necrotic tissue, associated with venous obstruction.
1. `Gas gangrene`: Caused by _Clostridium perfringens_ infection, producing gas in tissues.
1. `Fournier's gangrene`: A form of necrotizing fasciitis affecting perineum and genitalia.
1. `Meleney's synergistic gangrene`: Postoperative gangrene due to polymicrobial infection.

## Causes

### 1. Vascular Causes (Most Common)

- **Peripheral arterial disease (PAD)** → Chronic ischemia due to atherosclerosis.
- **Diabetes mellitus** → Microvascular and macrovascular complications.
- **Embolism or thrombosis** → Sudden arterial occlusion (e.g., atrial fibrillation, hypercoagulable states).
- **Aortic dissection or aneurysm** → Disrupts blood supply to lower limbs.

### 2. Infectious Causes

- **Polymicrobial infections** (wet gangrene) → Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, anaerobes.
- **Clostridial infection (gas gangrene)** → _Clostridium perfringens_ (trauma, surgery).
- **Necrotizing fasciitis** → Rapidly spreading soft tissue infection.
- **Fournier's gangrene** → Perineal polymicrobial infection.

### 3. Trauma & Mechanical Causes

- **Crush injury** → Vascular damage + compartment syndrome.
- **Frostbite** → Severe vasoconstriction → ischemia.
- **Severe burns** → Vascular thrombosis → necrosis.
- **Radiation injury** → Progressive tissue destruction due to endarteritis.

### 4. Iatrogenic Causes

- **Post-surgical vascular injury** → Inadvertent arterial ligation.
- **Improperly managed tight casts, tourniquets** → Compartment syndrome.
- **Long-standing IV drug use** → Local vasospasm and necrosis.

### 5. Autoimmune & Hematologic Causes

- **Vasculitis (e.g., Buerger's disease, SLE, polyarteritis nodosa)** → Arterial inflammation and thrombosis.
- **Sickle cell disease** → Vaso-occlusion → ischemia and necrosis.
- **DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation)** → Microvascular thrombosis.

## Management

### Dry Gangrene

#### Investigation [#DG]

- **Doppler ultrasound**: To assess blood flow.
- **CT/MRI angiography**: To locate the arterial occlusion.
- **ABPI (Ankle Brachial Pressure Index)**: To assess peripheral arterial disease.

#### Treatment [#DG]

- **Conservative** (for small, self-limiting dry gangrene):
  - Keep dry and avoid secondary infection.
  - Optimize blood glucose (in diabetics).
  - Improve circulation (e.g., smoking cessation, exercise).
- **Surgical**:
  - `Revascularization`:
    - `Endovascular (angioplasty + stenting)` if the lesion is amenable.
    - `Open bypass surgery` if extensive vascular occlusion.
  - `Amputation` if the gangrene is extensive or cannot be salvaged.

### Wet Gangrene

#### Investigation [#WG]

- **Wound swab culture & blood culture**: To identify causative bacteria.
- **X-ray**: To check for soft tissue gas.
- **CT scan**: If deep tissue involvement is suspected.
- **Inflammatory markers**: High WBC, CRP.

#### Treatment [#WG]

- **Emergency surgical debridement** to remove necrotic tissue
- **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics** (e.g., `piperacillin-tazobactam` ± `metronidazole`).
- **Amputation** may be required if infection spreads.
- **Revascularization** if ischemia contributes to gangrene.

### Gas Gangrene

#### Investigation [#GG]

- **X-ray**: Shows gas in soft tissues.
- **CT scan**: Determines tissue extent of gas collection.
- **Anaerobic blood culture & wound culture**: To confirm _Clostridium perfringens_.

#### Treatment [#GG]

- **Emergency fasciotomy & debridement** (delayed closure to prevent reinfection).
- `IV Penicillin G + Clindamycin` (`clindamycin` inhibits toxin production).
- **Hyperbaric oxygen therapy** (HBO) if available.
- **Amputation** in extensive involvement.

### Fournier's Gangrene

#### Investigation [#FG]

- **CT scan**: To assess extent and presence of gas.
- **Wound culture & blood culture**: To identify organisms.

#### Treatment [#FG]

- **Urgent surgical debridement** (multiple sessions if needed).
- **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics** (`carbapenem` + `clindamycin`).
- **Colostomy** if perianal involvement (to divert feces).
- **Reconstructive surgery** after infection control.

### Meleney's Synergistic Gangrene

#### Investigation [#MSG]

- **Wound culture**: Usually _Streptococcus_ + anaerobes.
- **MRI**: If deeper tissue involvement suspected.

#### Treatment [#MSG]

- **Serial debridement** (progresses slowly but requires repeated removal).
- **IV antibiotics** based on culture.
- **Flap reconstruction** after infection control.

<details>
<summary>Indications for amputation</summary>

1. Dead limb
   - Gangrene
1. Deadly limb
   - Wet gangrene
   - Spreading cellulitis
   - Arterovenous fistula
   - Neoplasm (e.g. Osteogenic sarcoma)
1. 'Dead loss' limb
   - Relentless severe rest pain
   - Limb impossible to use (paralysis, contracture)
   - Unrecoverable traumatic damage

</details>

<summary>Techniques used in gangrene management</summary>

1. Debridement Techniques
   - Sharp surgical debridement
   - Fasciotomy
   - Enzymatic debridement
   - Maggot therapy
2. Amputation Techniques
   - Guillotine amputation
   - Standard amputation (planned)
   - Re-amputation
3. Revascularization Techniques
   - Angioplasty + Stenting
   - Bypass Surgery
   - Endarterectomy
   - Thrombectomy or Embolectomy
4. Flap Reconstruction & Wound Closure
   - Delayed primary closure
   - Split-thickness skin graft (STSG)
   - Local or free flaps
   - Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

## Buerger's Disease (Thromboangiitis Obliterans)

> `Buerger's disease` is a **non-atherosclerotic, inflammatory occlusive vasculitis** affecting **small and medium arteries & veins**, mostly in **young male smokers**.

<details>
<summary>Pathophysiology</summary>

1. **Tobacco use** triggers **immune-mediated vascular inflammation**.
1. **Inflammation → thrombosis** (clot formation) → **occlusion** of arteries.
1. **Tissue ischemia** → **gangrene of fingers & toes**.
1. **Superficial thrombophlebitis** and **Raynaud's phenomenon** may occur.

</details>

### Clinical Features

- **Intermittent claudication** (pain in hands/feet with activity).
- **Rest pain in severe cases**.
- **Cold sensitivity (Raynaud's-like symptoms)**.
- **Ulcers or gangrene in fingers/toes**.
- **Migratory superficial thrombophlebitis** (red, tender veins).

### Diagnosis

- **Clinical criteria (Shionoya's Criteria)**:
  - Age < 50 years.
  - History of smoking.
  - Distal extremity ischemia (claudication, gangrene).
  - No other arterial occlusive diseases (e.g., atherosclerosis).
  - No hypercoagulable state.
- **Doppler ultrasound & Angiography**:
  - Shows **"corkscrew collaterals"** due to compensatory vessels.

### Management of Buerger's Disease

1. Smoking Cessation (Most Important)
1. **Vasodilators**: `Iloprost` (Prostacyclin analog), `Calcium channel blockers` (Nifedipine) for Raynaud's symptoms
1. **Antiplatelets** (`Aspirin`, `Clopidogrel`) to reduce clotting risk.
1. Pain & Ulcer Management
   - `Analgesics` (NSAIDs, opioids if severe).
   - **Wound care & debridement** for ulcers.
1. Amputation (For Gangrene)
