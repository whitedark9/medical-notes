# Urology Causes

## Causes of Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)

> Acute urinary retention (AUR) is the **sudden inability to pass urine**, leading to painful bladder distension.

<details>
<summary>Causes of Acute Urinary Retention</summary>

### 1. Obstructive Causes

1. **Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)**: most common in older males
1. **Urethral stricture**: due to infection, trauma, or instrumentation
1. **Bladder neck obstruction**: post-surgical scarring, congenital defects
1. **Prostate cancer**: advanced cases causing outflow obstruction
1. **Bladder stones**: blocking the bladder outlet
1. **Urethral calculus**: sudden blockage in the urethra
1. **Pelvic masses**: tumors compressing the bladder or urethra

### 2. Neurological Causes

1. **Spinal cord injury**: affects detrusor muscle control
1. **Multiple sclerosis**: demyelination affects bladder control
1. **Diabetic neuropathy**: damages autonomic control of urination
1. **Guillain-Barré syndrome**: acute paralysis affecting bladder function
1. **Parkinson's disease**: autonomic dysfunction affecting bladder emptying

### 3. Infective & Inflammatory Causes

1. **Urinary tract infection (UTI)**: severe inflammation and edema
1. **Prostatitis**: acute bacterial prostatitis causing swelling and obstruction
1. **Urethritis**: gonococcal or non-gonococcal infections
1. **Cystitis**: severe bladder inflammation causing detrusor dysfunction

### 4. Pharmacological Causes

(Medications causing urinary retention by affecting detrusor muscle or sphincter tone)

1. **Anticholinergics**: atropine, antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants
1. **Opioids**: morphine, codeine, fentanyl
1. **Alpha-adrenergic agonists**: pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine
1. **Calcium channel blockers**: verapamil, diltiazem
1. **NSAIDs**: ibuprofen, naproxen (rare)

### 5. Postoperative & Traumatic Causes

1. **Postoperative retention**: after spinal/epidural anesthesia, pelvic surgery
1. **Pelvic trauma**: urethral or bladder injury
1. **Prolonged catheterization**: detrusor dysfunction after removal

### 6. Functional & Psychogenic Causes

1. **Fowler's syndrome**: dysfunction of the urethral sphincter in young women
1. **Severe anxiety**: inhibits voluntary micturition reflex

### 7. Congenital Causes (Rare)

1. **Posterior urethral valves**: in newborn boys
1. **Meatal stenosis**: narrowing of the external urethral opening

</details>

## Causes of Haematuria

> Haematuria is the presence of red blood cells (RBCs) in urine, visible or detected microscopically.

<details>
<summary>Causes of haematuria</summary>

### Kidney (Renal causes)

- Renal cell carcinoma (most important surgical cause)
- Wilms’ tumour (in children)
- Angiomyolipoma
- Simple renal cyst rupture
- Polycystic kidney disease (PKD)
- Trauma (renal laceration or contusion)
- Renal infarction

### Ureter

- Urothelial carcinoma of ureter (transitional cell carcinoma)
- Ureteric stone
- Ureteric trauma (iatrogenic, penetrating, blunt)

### Bladder

- Bladder cancer (transitional cell carcinoma is common)
- Radiation cystitis
- Schistosomiasis (→ squamous cell carcinoma of bladder)
- Bladder stones
- Trauma (e.g. pelvic fracture → bladder rupture)

### Prostate (in males)

- Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)
- Prostate cancer
- Prostatic trauma (e.g. catheterisation)

### Urethra

- Urethral stricture
- Urethral trauma (e.g. pelvic fracture, catheter-related injury)
- Urethral carcinoma

</details>

## Causes of Urolithiasis

<details>
<summary>Causes of Urolithiasis</summary>

### 1. Metabolic Causes

1. **Hypercalciuria** → Increased urinary calcium excretion (most common)
   - Primary hyperparathyroidism
   - Sarcoidosis, Vitamin D toxicity
   - High dietary calcium intake
1. **Hyperoxaluria** → Increased oxalate excretion
   - Excess dietary oxalate (spinach, nuts, chocolate)
   - Malabsorption (Crohn's disease, short bowel syndrome)
1. **Hyperuricosuria** → Increased uric acid in urine
   - Gout, high purine diet (red meat, seafood)
1. **Hypocitraturia** → Decreased urinary citrate (citrate prevents st one formation)
   - Chronic metabolic acidosis (renal tubular acidosis, diarrhea)

### 2. Infectious Causes

- **Struvite stones (Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate stones)**
  - Caused by **urease-producing bacteria** (Proteus, Klebsiella)
  - Leads to **alkaline urine** → stone formation

### 3. Structural & Functional Causes

- **Urinary stasis** → Promotes stone formation
  - **Obstruction** (e.g., ureteropelvic junction obstruction, BPH)
  - **Neurogenic bladder**
- **Medullary sponge kidney** → Congenital cystic dilation of renal tubules

### 4. Drug-Induced Causes

- **Drugs that crystallize in urine** → `Acyclovir`, `Indinavir`, `Sulfonamides`, `Triamterene`

### 5. Dietary & Lifestyle Factors

- **Low fluid intake** → Concentrated urine promotes stone formation
- **High sodium intake** → Increases calcium excretion
- **High oxalate diet** → Increases oxalate levels (spinach, rhubarb, tea)

</details>

## Reanl Injury

<details>

<summary>American Association for the Surgery of Trauma (AAST)</summary>

### Grade I - Minor Injury

- Renal contusion or non-expanding subcapsular `hematoma`
- `No laceration`

### Grade II - Mild Injury

- Non-expanding perirenal `hematoma`
- < 1cm parenchymal `laceration`, no urinary extravasation

### Grade III - Moderate Injury

- `Laceration` > 1cm depth into cortex
- No collecting system injury

### Grade IV - Severe Injury

- `Laceration` extending into collecting system → urinary extravasation
- Segmental renal artery/vein injury with `contained hemorrhage`

### Grade V - Critical Injury

- `Shattered kidney`
- Avulsion of renal hilum → devascularized kidney

</details>
