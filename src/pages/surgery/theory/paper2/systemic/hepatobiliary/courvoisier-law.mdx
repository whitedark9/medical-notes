# Courvoisier's Law (Courvoisier’s Sign)

> In the presence of a palpable, non-tender gallbladder and jaundice, the cause is unlikely to be gallstones.

## Interpretation

| Finding                                | Likely Cause                                                             |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Palpable, non-tender GB + jaundice** | **Malignancy** (e.g. periampullary, pancreatic head, cholangiocarcinoma) |
| **Non-palpable GB + jaundice**         | More likely due to **gallstones** (chronic inflammation → fibrotic GB)   |

## Pathophysiological Basis

- **Gallstones** → chronic cholecystitis → fibrosis & contraction of gallbladder → **not palpable**
- **Malignant obstruction** (e.g. pancreatic head cancer) → **gradual bile duct blockage** → distended, **palpable gallbladder** (no inflammation)

→ **Painless jaundice + palpable GB** → think **malignancy**

## Exceptions to the Law

- **Double pathology:** stone + cancer
- **Mirizzi syndrome** (gallstone compressing CBD but GB still distended)
- **Acute acalculous cholecystitis**
- **Carcinoma of gallbladder** with stones

## Associated Signs

| Sign              | Suggestion                       |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Murphy's sign** | Acute cholecystitis (tender GB)  |
| **Courvoisier's** | Malignant obstruction (painless) |
| **Boas sign**     | Gallbladder disease (see below)  |

## Boas Sign (for comparison)

> Hyperaesthesia (increased sensitivity) below **right scapula**.

- **Seen in:** Gallbladder disease, especially **cholecystitis**
- **Mechanism:** Referred pain via phrenic nerve (C3–C5)
