## Types of Dysphagia (based on cause):

### 1. Oropharyngeal Dysphagia

- Cause: Difficulty initiating swallowing (transfer from mouth to esophagus)
- Common etiologies:
  - Neurological: stroke, Parkinson's disease, ALS, MS
  - Muscular: myasthenia gravis, muscular dystrophies
  - Structural: Zenker's diverticulum, cervical osteophytes
  - Iatrogenic: surgery or radiation to head/neck

### 2. Esophageal Dysphagia

- Cause: Difficulty with bolus transit through the esophagus

#### A. Dynamic (Motility-related) Causes

_“Dynamic” refers to impaired peristalsis or muscle function_

- Primary motility disorders:
  1. Achalasia
     - LES fails to relax → aperistalsis of esophageal body
  2. Diffuse esophageal spasm
     - Uncoordinated contractions → intermittent dysphagia ± chest pain
  3. Nutcracker esophagus
     - High amplitude but coordinated contractions
- Secondary motility disorders:
  - Scleroderma → smooth muscle atrophy → absent peristalsis + low LES tone
  - Diabetes (autonomic neuropathy)
  - Chagas disease

#### B. Adynamic (Obstructive or Structural) Causes

_“Adynamic” refers to mechanical blockage, not related to muscle movement_

- Intraluminal:
  1. Foreign body
  2. Food bolus impaction
- Intramural:
  1. Esophageal carcinoma
  2. Peptic stricture (from GERD)
  3. Schatzki’s ring
  4. Esophageal web (Plummer-Vinson syndrome)¹
- Extrinsic compression:
  - Enlarged left atrium (mitral stenosis)
  - Aortic aneurysm
  - Mediastinal tumors or lymphadenopathy
  - Cervical spine osteophytes

## Clinical Clues to Type of Dysphagia

| Feature                           | Oropharyngeal                  | Esophageal |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ---------- |
| Difficulty initiating swallow     | Yes                            | No         |
| Coughing/choking/regurgitation    | Common                         | Rare       |
| Nasal regurgitation               | Common                         | Rare       |
| Localisation of discomfort        | Neck                           | Chest      |
| Association with solids only      | Suggests mechanical (adynamic) |            |
| Association with solids + liquids | Suggests motility (dynamic)    |            |

## Management of Carcinoma Oesophagus at 22 cm

### Important Characteristics

- **Location**: Upper thoracic oesophagus
- **Histology**: Most commonly **squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)**
- **Common symptoms**: Progressive dysphagia, weight loss, odynophagia
- **Surgical complexity**: Close proximity to trachea, major vessels → limits surgical access
- **Preferred treatment**: Often **non-surgical** due to anatomical constraints

### 1. **Investigation**

#### **History**

- Progressive dysphagia (initially solids → later liquids)
- Odynophagia
- Weight loss
- Voice hoarseness (suggests recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement)

#### **Physical Examination**

- Nutritional status
- Signs of metastasis (e.g. supraclavicular lymphadenopathy)
- Airway compromise (stridor)

#### **Laboratory Tests**

- CBC, LFTs
- Tumour markers (not specific but may support diagnosis)

#### **Imaging**

1. **Upper GI endoscopy**

   - Confirms tumour at 22 cm
   - Allows biopsy for histology

2. **CT thorax-abdomen-pelvis**

   - Staging (T, N, M)
   - Evaluate resectability

3. **EUS (Endoscopic ultrasound)**

   - Assess depth of invasion (T stage) and regional nodes

4. **PET-CT**

   - Detect distant metastases

5. **Bronchoscopy**

   - Important due to proximity to tracheobronchial tree

### 2. **Treatment**

#### A. **Surgical Management**

Surgery is **technically difficult** and **not first-line** for tumours at 22 cm due to:

- Close relation to trachea, aortic arch, recurrent laryngeal nerve
- Narrow operative field in upper thorax

##### **Surgery Indications** (if considered):

- Fit patient
- Resectable tumour (T1–T3, N0–1, M0)
- No tracheobronchial involvement

##### **Preoperative Preparation**

- Nutritional support (NG or feeding jejunostomy if needed)
- Prehabilitation
- Neoadjuvant CRT (CROSS protocol: carboplatin + paclitaxel + RT)

#### **Surgical Options (Rare)**

##### 1. **Three-Stage Oesophagectomy (McKeown procedure)**

- **Approach**:

  - **Abdomen** (laparotomy) – gastric mobilisation
  - **Right thoracotomy** – oesophageal mobilisation
  - **Neck** – cervical oesophagogastrostomy

- **Advantage**: Avoids intrathoracic anastomosis (safer in high lesions)
- **Preferred for high thoracic and cervical tumours**

##### 2. **Transhiatal Oesophagectomy (THE)**

- No thoracotomy (abdomen + neck)
- **Limitations**: Poor exposure in upper thoracic region

#### B. **Non-surgical Treatment (Mainstay)**

##### Definitive Concurrent Chemoradiotherapy (CRT)

- **Preferred for tumours at 22 cm**
- Radiation (50–60 Gy) + chemotherapy (cisplatin + 5-FU or carboplatin + paclitaxel)

#### C. **Palliative Options** (advanced or metastatic disease)

- Self-expanding metallic stent (SEMS) for dysphagia
- Laser/argon plasma coagulation if tumour is obstructive
- Pain and nutritional management
- Palliative RT ± chemo

### 3. **Postoperative Complications (if surgery is done)**

- **Anastomotic leak** (esp. in cervical anastomosis)
- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy** → hoarseness
- **Respiratory complications** (e.g. pneumonia)
- **Chylothorax** (injury to thoracic duct)
- **Stricture formation**

### 4. **Prognosis**

- 5-year survival:

  - **Surgery alone**: \~20–30%
  - **Neoadjuvant CRT + surgery**: \~35–40%
  - **CRT alone**: \~20–30%
