# Gallstone Pancreatitis

> Gallstone pancreatitis is **acute inflammation of the pancreas** due to gallstone obstruction of the common bile duct or pancreatic duct, causing enzyme autoactivation.

## History

- Sudden-onset epigastric pain radiating to the back
- Nausea, vomiting
- History of gallstones, fatty meals

## Physical Examination

- Tender epigastrium, guarding
- Fever, tachycardia
- Jaundice (if CBD obstruction)

## Investigation

### Laboratory Tests

- **Serum amylase/lipase**: ↑ (>3× normal)
- **LFTs**:
  - ALT > 150 U/L → suggests gallstone pancreatitis
  - ↑ Bilirubin, ALP if CBD obstruction
- **FBC**: leukocytosis
- **U\&E**: check for AKI
- **CRP**: severity marker
- **Calcium**: ↓ in severe cases
- **Blood glucose**: ↑ due to islet damage

### Imaging

- **Transabdominal ultrasound**:
  - Gallstones, CBD dilation
- **MRCP** or **EUS**:
  - If ultrasound inconclusive
- **CT abdomen (with contrast)**:
  - After 48–72 h if worsening or unclear diagnosis
  - Detects necrosis or complications

## Emergency Management

### Airway

- Ensure patent airway
- Consider intubation if decreased consciousness or aspiration risk

### Breathing

- Oxygen supplementation to maintain SpO₂ > 94%
- Monitor for signs of respiratory distress or ARDS (acute respiratory distress syndrome)

### Circulation

- IV fluids: aggressive resuscitation with **Ringer's lactate**
- Monitor vitals, urine output, hematocrit
- Correct electrolyte imbalances

### Disability

- Assess neurological status (GCS)
- Monitor for signs of hypocalcemia (confusion, tetany)

### Exposure

- Full examination: look for Grey-Turner or Cullen's sign (signs of hemorrhagic pancreatitis)
- Rectal exam if GI bleeding suspected

### Fluids

- Maintain adequate hydration (4-6 L/day in first 24 h)
- Avoid overhydration (monitor for pulmonary edema)

## Management

### Supportive Care

- NPO (nil per os) initially → start oral feeding once pain and inflammation subside
- Analgesia (e.g., IV opioids)
- Anti-emetics

### Biliary Decompression

- **ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography)**:
  - **Indicated if**:
    - Persistent biliary obstruction (↑ bilirubin, ALP)
    - Cholangitis (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain)
    - CBD stone seen on imaging
  - Ideally within **24–72 hours**

### Cholecystectomy

- **Definitive treatment** to prevent recurrence
- **Timing**:
  - **Mild pancreatitis**: **early cholecystectomy during same admission**
  - **Severe pancreatitis**: delayed until recovery and resolution of inflammation

## Complications

- **Pancreatic necrosis**
- **Infected pancreatic collections/abscess**
- **Pseudocyst**
- **ARDS**
- **Renal failure**
- **Sepsis**

## Management of Complications

- **Necrosis**:
  - Antibiotics **only if infected**
  - Debridement (endoscopic/surgical) if unstable or infected
- **Pseudocyst**:
  - Drain if >6 cm or symptomatic
- **ARDS/AKI**:
  - ICU management

## Prevention

- **Early cholecystectomy**
- Avoid alcohol if contributory
- Monitor and manage lipid levels if hypertriglyceridemia

## Prognosis

- **Mild cases**: recover in 3–5 days
- **Severe cases**: mortality up to 20–30% with complications
- **Ranson's criteria** or **Revised Atlanta Classification** used for prognosis
