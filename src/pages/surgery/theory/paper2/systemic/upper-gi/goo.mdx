# Gastric Outlet Obstruction (GOO)

> A condition where there is obstruction at the distal stomach, pylorus, or proximal duodenum, preventing gastric emptying.

## Causes

- **Benign Causes**
  1. Peptic ulcer disease (PUD)` → Chronic inflammation → Scarring & fibrosis of the pylorus
  1. Gastric bezoars` → Indigestible material accumulation
  1. Pyloric stenosis` (in infants) → Hypertrophy of the pylorus muscle
  1. Crohn's disease` → Inflammatory strictures in the duodenum
  1. Duodenal web` → Congenital incomplete obstruction
- **Malignant Causes**
  1. Gastric carcinoma` → Tumor at antrum or pylorus
  1. Pancreatic cancer` → Compression from the pancreatic head
  1. Duodenal cancer` → Intraluminal obstruction
  1. Lymphoma` → Infiltrating the gastric or duodenal wall

<summary>Fluid balance</summary>

- Maintained by **intake (oral, IV fluids) and output (urine, sweat, feces, respiration)**
- **Regulated by**
  - `Osmoreceptors` (hypothalamus) → Thirst mechanism
  - `Antidiuretic hormone (ADH)` → Water retention
  - `Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)` → Sodium & water retention
  - `Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP)` → Sodium & water excretion

</details>

## Fluid and Electrolyte Changes in Patients with GOO

### Pathophysiology

- `GOO` → Vomiting (loss of gastric contents) → Loss of **hydrochloric acid (HCl)**
- **Dehydration** → Hypovolemia (due to fluid loss)
- **Electrolyte Imbalances**
  - **Metabolic alkalosis** → Due to HCl loss
  - **Hypokalemia** (`K+ loss`) → Due to vomiting & renal compensation
  - **Hypochloremia** (`Cl- loss`) → Due to HCl depletion
  - **Hyponatremia** (`Na+ loss`) → Due to fluid loss & renal compensation

### Compensatory Mechanisms

- **Kidneys retain Na+ and HCO₃-** → Worsens metabolic alkalosis
- **RAAS activation** → ↑ Aldosterone → ↑ Na+ retention but ↑ K+ loss
- **ADH secretion** → Water retention → Can lead to dilutional hyponatremia

## Diagnosis

### 1. History

- **Symptoms**

  - Persistent **non-bilious vomiting** (partially digested food, no bile)
  - **Early satiety**, postprandial fullness
  - **Weight loss** (if malignancy)
  - **Epigastric pain** (ulcer-related or malignancy-related)
  - **Hematemesis** (if ulcer bleeds)

- **Risk Factors**
  - `Chronic peptic ulcer disease (PUD)` → Scarring, fibrosis
  - `Gastric cancer` → Tumor blocking the pylorus
  - `NSAID use` → Peptic ulcer formation
  - `Helicobacter pylori` infection

### 2. Physical Examination

- **Visible peristalsis** (left to right waves in the epigastrium)
- **Succussion splash** (sloshing sound when shaking abdomen) → Indicates retained gastric contents
- **Palpable epigastric mass** (if malignancy)
- **Signs of dehydration** (dry mucosa, tachycardia, hypotension)

### 3. Investigations

#### Laboratory Tests

- **Electrolytes** → Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypochloremia
- **ABG** → Metabolic alkalosis (due to HCl loss)
- **CBC** → Anemia (if malignancy or chronic PUD)
- **H. pylori testing** → If PUD is suspected
- **Tumor markers** (CEA, CA 19-9) → If malignancy suspected

#### Imaging

- **X-ray (Abdomen, Erect)**

  - Dilated stomach with air-fluid levels
  - No gas in the small intestine (suggesting obstruction)

- **Ultrasound (Abdomen)**

  - Detects thickened gastric wall or mass

- **Upper GI Endoscopy** (**Gold standard**)

  - Identifies **cause** (ulcer, malignancy, mass, stricture)
  - **Biopsy** if malignancy suspected

- **CT Abdomen**

  - Evaluates extent of **gastric cancer** or extrinsic compression
  - Staging of malignancy

- **Barium Meal** (if endoscopy not available)
  - **"String sign"** (narrowed pylorus due to scarring)
  - **"Shouldering sign"** (irregular narrowing, suggests malignancy)

## Management of GOO

### 1. Initial Stabilization

- **NPO (Nil per os)** → Prevent further vomiting
- **NG Tube Decompression** → Relieves gastric distension
- **IV Fluids (0.9% NaCl + KCl)** → Corrects dehydration & electrolyte imbalances
- **Electrolyte Replacement**
  - KCl for hypokalemia
  - NaCl for hyponatremia & hypochloremia
