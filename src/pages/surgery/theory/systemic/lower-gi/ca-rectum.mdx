# Carcinoma of Rectum

> A **rectosigmoid tumor** is a malignancy at the junction of the rectum and sigmoid colon. When it causes **acute intestinal obstruction**, it presents as a **surgical emergency** due to **mechanical blockage** of colonic contents.

<details>
<summary>Rectum parts</summary>

Rectum (≈12–15 cm in length) is divided into three parts based on distance from the anal verge (AV):

1. **Upper third**:
   - 10–15 cm from AV
   - Peritoneal covering anteriorly and laterally
2. **Middle third**:
   - 5–10 cm from AV
   - Peritoneum only anteriorly
3. **Lower third**:
   - 0–5 cm from AV
   - Extraperitoneal

</details>

<details>
<summary>Intestinal obstruction causes </summary>

## Mechanical Causes

### Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

Most common site of obstruction.

1. **Adhesions** (most common in developed countries)
   - Postoperative fibrous bands
1. **Hernias** (e.g., inguinal, femoral, incisional)
   - Bowel loops trapped in a hernia sac
1. **Intussusception**
   - Telescoping of one part of bowel into another
1. **Volvulus**
   - Twisting of the intestine, commonly midgut
1. **Neoplasms**
   - Intramural or extrinsic compression (rare in SBO)
1. **Strictures**
   - Crohn's disease, radiation, ischemia
1. **Gallstone ileus**
   - Large gallstone erodes into bowel and obstructs (usually ileum)
1. **Foreign bodies / bezoars**

### Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)

1. **Colorectal carcinoma** (most common in elderly)
   - Tumor blocks lumen
1. **Diverticulitis with stricture formation**
1. **Volvulus**
   - Commonly sigmoid or caecal
1. **Fecal impaction**
1. **Benign strictures** (e.g., IBD, radiation)
1. **Pseudo-obstruction** (see below)

## Functional (Non-mechanical) Causes

### Paralytic Ileus

→ Failure of peristalsis without physical blockage

1. Postoperative state
1. Electrolyte imbalance (e.g., ↓K⁺, ↓Mg²⁺)
1. Drugs (e.g., opioids, anticholinergics)
1. Peritonitis, pancreatitis, sepsis

### Ogilvie Syndrome (Acute Colonic Pseudo-obstruction)

→ Massive colonic dilatation without true obstruction

1. Often in hospitalized, elderly or immobile patients

## Congenital Causes

(Usually present in neonates or children)

1. **Duodenal atresia**
   - Congenital absence of lumen
1. **Meconium ileus**
   - Associated with cystic fibrosis
1. **Hirschsprung’s disease**
   - Aganglionosis → functional obstruction
1. **Malrotation with volvulus**

## Others / Rare Causes

1. **Endometriosis**
   - Implants causing strictures
1. **Tuberculosis**
   - Ileocaecal strictures
1. **Mesenteric ischemia**
   - Late stage can mimic obstruction
1. **Radiation enteritis**

## Clinical Features

### A. Symptoms

1. **Abdominal pain** – colicky, progressive, usually lower abdomen
2. **Abdominal distension** – more marked in distal obstruction
3. **Absolute constipation** – no passage of flatus or stool
4. **Vomiting** – late feature (feculent if prolonged)
5. **Weight loss**, anorexia (suggest malignancy)
6. **Altered bowel habits** – alternating constipation and diarrhea
7. **Rectal bleeding** – fresh blood or melena

### B. **Signs**

1. **Distended abdomen** – tympanic on percussion
2. **Hyperactive or absent bowel sounds**
3. **Empty rectum on digital rectal examination**
4. **Palpable mass** – may be felt per abdomen or per rectum
5. **Dehydration, tachycardia, hypotension** (if late/severe)

## Investigations

### A. Laboratory

1. **CBC**: Anemia (iron-deficiency), leukocytosis if perforation/necrosis
2. **Electrolytes, urea, creatinine**: Assess dehydration and renal function
3. **LFTs**: Rule out liver metastases
4. **CEA (Carcinoembryonic antigen)**: Tumor marker, baseline for monitoring
5. **Blood grouping and cross-match** (if surgery planned)

### B. Imaging

1. **Plain abdominal X-ray (erect and supine)**
   - Dilated colon with air-fluid levels
   - Cut-off at rectosigmoid region
2. **Contrast-enhanced CT (CECT) abdomen-pelvis**
   - Confirms site of obstruction
   - Identifies tumor, perforation, metastases
3. **Water-soluble contrast enema** _(optional)_
   - "Apple-core" or "napkin ring" lesion

### C. Endoscopy (after stabilization)

- Flexible sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy to visualize tumor and take biopsy (if not obstructed fully)

<summary>Staging of colon-rectal carcinoma (CRC)</summary>

### TNM Classification

#### T – Tumor (Depth of Invasion)

1. **Tis**: Carcinoma in situ (intraepithelial or lamina propria only)
1. **T1**: Invades submucosa
1. **T2**: Invades muscularis propria
1. **T3**: Invades through muscularis propria into subserosa or non1.peritonealized pericolic/perirectal tissues
1. **T4a**: Penetrates visceral peritoneum
1. **T4b**: Invades or adheres to adjacent organs or structures

#### N – Regional Lymph Nodes

1. **N0**: No regional lymph node metastasis
1. **N1**: 1–3 regional lymph nodes involved
   - **N1a**: 1 node
   - **N1b**: 2–3 nodes
   - **N1c**: Tumor deposits in subserosa, mesentery, or non-peritonealized tissue without lymph node involvement
1. **N2**: ≥4 regional lymph nodes involved
   - **N2a**: 4–6 nodes
   - **N2b**: ≥7 nodes

#### M – Distant Metastasis

1. **M0**: No distant metastasis
1. **M1**: Distant metastasis
   - **M1a**: One site/organ (e.g., liver, lung)
   - **M1b**: >1 site/organ
   - **M1c**: Peritoneal metastasis ± other sites

### AJCC/UICC Stages (Group Stage)

| Stage          | TNM Description                  |
| -------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Stage 0**    | Tis N0 M0                        |
| **Stage I**    | T1–T2 N0 M0                      |
| **Stage IIA**  | T3 N0 M0                         |
| **Stage IIB**  | T4a N0 M0                        |
| **Stage IIC**  | T4b N0 M0                        |
| **Stage IIIA** | T1–T2 N1/N1c M0                  |
| **Stage IIIB** | T3–T4a N1/N1c M0 or T2–T3 N2a M0 |
| **Stage IIIC** | T4a–T4b N2a/N2b M0               |
| **Stage IVA**  | Any T, Any N, M1a                |
| **Stage IVB**  | Any T, Any N, M1b                |
| **Stage IVC**  | Any T, Any N, M1c                |

### Importance of Staging

- Guides treatment (surgery ± chemotherapy ± radiotherapy)
- Predicts prognosis (5-year survival):
  - Stage I: >90%
  - Stage II: \~70–85%
  - Stage III: \~40–60%
  - Stage IV: < 20%

## Management (Elective Surgery)

### A. Upper Rectal Cancer (10–15 cm)

- Surgery:
  - `High anterior resection (HAR)` with `Total Mesorectal Excision (TME)`
- Oncologic Therapy:
  - **Stage I (T1–T2, N0)**: Surgery alone
  - **Stage II–III (T3–T4, N+)**:
    - `Neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy (CRT)` → `HAR` → `Adjuvant chemotherapy`

### B. Middle Rectal Cancer (5–10 cm)

- Surgery:
  - `Low anterior resection (LAR)` with `TME`
  - `Protective ileostomy` may be needed due to low anastomosis
- Oncologic Therapy:
  - **Stage I**: LAR alone
  - **Stage II–III**: Neoadjuvant CRT → LAR → Adjuvant chemotherapy

### C. Lower Rectal Cancer (0–5 cm)

- Surgery Options:
  1. `LAR` with `coloanal anastomosis` (if sphincter preserved)
  2. `Abdominoperineal resection (APR)`:
     - If sphincter involved or not preservable
     - Requires permanent colostomy
  3. `Intersphincteric resection (ISR)`:
     - For very low tumors with preserved internal sphincter
- **Oncologic Therapy**:
  - T3/T4 or N+ tumors → Neoadjuvant CRT → Surgery → Adjuvant chemotherapy

## Non-Operable Tumors

### A. Locally Advanced, Non-Resectable

- **Neoadjuvant CRT**:
  - Long-course radiotherapy + 5-FU or capecitabine
  - Goal: Downstaging → re-evaluation for resection
- **If still unresectable**:
  - Consider palliative stoma or radiotherapy

### B. Metastatic (Stage IV)

- **Systemic chemotherapy**:
  - FOLFOX/FOLFIRI ± targeted therapy (bevacizumab, cetuximab\*)
  - `Cetuximab` only in RAS wild-type tumors
- **Palliative radiotherapy**:
  - For bleeding, pain, tenesmus
- **Palliative surgery**:
  - Diverting colostomy or Hartmann's procedure (for obstruction)

## Emergency Management

### Common Emergencies:

1. Obstruction
2. Perforation
3. Bleeding

4. A – Airway
   - Ensure patent airway
5. B – Breathing
   - Oxygen supplementation if needed
6. C – Circulation
   - IV fluids, blood products
   - Correct anemia/coagulopathy
7. D – Disability
   - Assess GCS if perforation with sepsis
8. E – Exposure
   - Examine abdomen, per rectal exam
9. F – Fluids & Definitive Care

### Emergency Surgical Options by Location

| Emergency                 | Location     | Possible Procedure                                                          |
| ------------------------- | ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Obstruction / Perforation | Any location | **Hartmann’s procedure** (resection + end colostomy + rectal stump closure) |
| Obstruction (distal)      | Lower rectum | **Defunctioning colostomy** (palliation)                                    |
| Perforated tumor          | Middle/Lower | **Hartmann’s procedure** or **APR** in fit patients                         |
| Bleeding (uncontrolled)   | Any location | **Urgent resection** or **palliative RT**                                   |

#### Hartmann’s Procedure

A two-stage colorectal surgery involving:

1.  Resection of the rectosigmoid colon
1.  Closure of the rectal stump
1.  Formation of an end colostomy

No primary anastomosis is performed in the initial operation.

### Summary Table by Location

| Tumor Site     | Elective Surgery             | Neoadjuvant CRT       | Emergency Option                        |
| -------------- | ---------------------------- | --------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Upper          | High anterior resection      | Stage II–III          | Hartmann’s if perforated/obstructed     |
| Middle         | Low anterior resection (LAR) | Stage II–III          | Hartmann’s procedure                    |
| Lower          | LAR / APR / ISR              | Always if T3/T4 or N+ | APR or Hartmann’s / Defunctioning stoma |
| Non-resectable | N/A                          | Always                | Palliative stoma / radiotherapy         |
| Metastatic     | Palliative resection/stoma   | Systemic chemo ± RT   | Same as above                           |
