# Severe Acute Pancreatitis (SAP)

> Severe acute pancreatitis is an acute inflammation of the pancreas associated with **organ failure**, **local or systemic complications**, and/or **necrosis**, often requiring intensive care.

## Causes

> I GET SMASHED

1. `I`diopathic
1. `G`allstones (most common)
1. `E`thanol (alcohol abuse)
1. `T`rauma
1. `S`teroids
1. `M`umps and other infections (e.g., Coxsackie virus)
1. `A`utoimmune (autoimmune pancreatitis)
1. `S`corpion sting (rare)
1. `H`ypercalcemia, Hyperlipidemia
1. `E`RCP (iatrogenic)
1. `D`rugs (azathioprine, thiazides, valproate, etc.)

## Pathology

### Pathophysiology

**Trigger (e.g., gallstone, alcohol) → pancreatic acinar cell injury → premature activation of trypsinogen to trypsin inside pancreas**
→ autodigestion of pancreatic tissue
→ local inflammation, necrosis, and hemorrhage
→ release of cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)
→ **SIRS (systemic inflammatory response syndrome)**
→ multiorgan dysfunction (lung, kidney, cardiovascular system)

### Types of Morphological Changes

- **Interstitial edematous pancreatitis**: mild, self-limiting
- **Necrotizing pancreatitis**: necrosis of parenchyma and/or peripancreatic tissue
- May lead to **infected necrosis**, **pancreatic abscess**, or **pseudocyst**

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

- Sudden, severe **epigastric pain** radiating to back
- **Nausea, vomiting**
- Aggravated by eating; relieved by sitting forward

### Signs

- **Fever**, **tachycardia**
- **Abdominal tenderness**, guarding
- **Hypotension** (due to third-spacing of fluid)
- **Grey-Turner's sign**: flank ecchymosis (retroperitoneal hemorrhage)
- **Cullen's sign**: periumbilical ecchymosis
- **Ileus** (absent bowel sounds)

### Signs of Severe Disease

- Hypoxia
- Oliguria/acute kidney injury
- Altered mental status
- Hypocalcemia (→ tetany)
- Persistent hypotension or shock

## Investigations

### Laboratory

- `↑ Serum amylase/lipase` (> 3x normal)
- `↑ WBC, CRP, procalcitonin`
- `↓ Calcium`
- `LFTs` (↑ ALT suggests gallstone pancreatitis)
- `ABG`: may show hypoxia or metabolic acidosis
- `BUN/Creatinine`: for AKI
- `Glucose`: hyperglycemia

### Imaging

- **CT abdomen with contrast**: for necrosis, pseudocyst, fluid collections
- **Ultrasound**: detect gallstones, biliary obstruction
- **MRCP/ERCP**: if biliary cause suspected

## Management

### Emergency Management

#### Airway

- Assess for airway compromise; intubate if GCS < 8 or respiratory failure

#### Breathing

- Oxygen supplementation or mechanical ventilation if PaO₂ < 60 mmHg

#### Circulation

- Large-bore IV access
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation (isotonic crystalloid, e.g., Ringer's lactate)
- Monitor urine output (goal: >0.5 mL/kg/h)
- Vasopressors if hypotension persists after fluids

#### Disability

- Assess GCS, blood glucose (can be ↑ due to islet cell injury)

#### Exposure

- Look for signs of ecchymosis (Grey-Turner, Cullen)

#### Fluid

- Maintain fluid balance
- Insert Foley catheter; monitor input/output

### Ochsner-Sherren Regimen

> A **conservative (non-operative) management protocol** historically used for **appendicular mass** (a complication of acute appendicitis where an inflamed appendix is walled off by omentum and bowel).

#### Indication

- **Appendicular mass** without signs of generalized peritonitis or sepsis
- Patient is clinically stable, no features of rupture or abscess formation requiring drainage

#### Components

1. **Nil by Mouth (NPO)**: To rest the bowel and prevent worsening of inflammation
1. **Nasogastric Tube (NGT)**: If vomiting or evidence of ileus
1. **IV Fluids**: Maintain hydration and correct electrolyte imbalances
1. **IV Antibiotics**: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone + metronidazole) to control infection and reduce inflammation
1. **Monitoring**
   - Vital signs every 4-6 hours
   - Abdominal examination daily
   - Monitor WBC and CRP
   - Assess for any signs of deterioration (fever, pain, sepsis)
1. **Analgesia**: Paracetamol or other suitable pain control agents

#### Expected Course

- Clinical improvement within **48-72 hours**
- Reduction in tenderness and mass size
- Fever subsides, WBC normalizes

#### Elective Surgery (Interval Appendectomy)

- Usually considered **6-8 weeks** after conservative management
- Rationale: To prevent recurrence, though increasingly debated in modern practice

#### Failure Criteria (Indications for Surgery)

- Persistent or worsening symptoms
- Signs of abscess formation or rupture
- Development of generalized peritonitis
- Clinical deterioration despite conservative management

#### Complications if Regimen Fails

- Generalized peritonitis
- Appendicular abscess
- Intestinal obstruction
- Sepsis

### Specific Treatments

- **Gallstone pancreatitis**:
  - ERCP with `sphincterotomy` (if cholangitis present)
  - `Cholecystectomy` after recovery
- **Infected necrosis**:
  - Antibiotics (e.g., `carbapenems`)
  - Percutaneous drainage
  - Necrosectomy (if needed)

## Complications

### Local

- Pancreatic necrosis (sterile or infected)
- Pancreatic pseudocyst
- Pancreatic abscess
- Ascites, hemorrhage

### Systemic

- Shock
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
- Acute kidney injury
- DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)
- Sepsis and multiorgan failure

## Prognosis

- Mortality of SAP \~20-30%
- Dependent on presence and persistence of organ failure
- **Scores for prognosis**:
  - **Ranson's Criteria**
  - **APACHE II**
  - **Modified Marshall Score**
  - **BISAP Score**

## Prevention

- **Avoid alcohol**
- **Early cholecystectomy** for gallstone pancreatitis
- **Avoid known trigger drugs**
- **Control hyperlipidemia, calcium disorders**
