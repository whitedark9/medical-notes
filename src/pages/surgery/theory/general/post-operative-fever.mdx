# Post-Operative Fever

> Fever (≥38.5°C) occurring after surgery, often due to inflammatory, infectious, or thrombotic causes.

> Classified based on timing: immediate (0-24h), early (1-3 days), intermediate (4-7 days), and late (>7 days)

## Causes by Post-Op Day (5Ws)

| Post-Op Day  | Cause                    | Description                                                                                                 |
| ------------ | ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **0-24h**    | `W`ind (Atelectasis)     | Alveolar collapse due to hypoventilation, pain, or anesthesia effects. Rarely causes fever but often blamed |
| **1-3 days** | `W`ater (UTI)            | Common in patients with urinary catheters                                                                   |
| **4-7 days** | `W`ound Infection        | Superficial or deep surgical site infection (SSI)                                                           |
| **5-7 days** | `W`alking (DVT/PE)       | Venous stasis leading to deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or pulmonary embolism (PE)                              |
| **> 7 days** | `W`onder Drugs / Abscess | Drug fever or deep abscess (intra-abdominal, pelvic, or anastomotic leak)                                   |

## Diagnosis

### 1. History

- **Surgical details**: Type of surgery, intra-op complications, prosthetic implants, drains, etc.
- **Pre-existing conditions**: Diabetes, malignancy, immunosuppression.
- **Post-op factors**: Catheters, lines, ventilators, wound care, mobility.

### 2. Physical Examination

- **Vitals**: Fever pattern, hemodynamic stability.
- **Surgical site**: Redness, warmth, purulent drainage.
- **Lungs**: Crackles (atelectasis, pneumonia).
- **Abdomen**: Distension, tenderness (abscess, anastomotic leak).
- **Legs**: Swelling, pain (DVT).

### 3. Investigations

- **Blood tests**: CBC (leukocytosis), CRP, blood cultures if sepsis suspected.
- **Urine analysis & culture**: If UTI suspected.
- **Chest X-ray**: Pneumonia, atelectasis.
- **Ultrasound/Doppler**: Suspected DVT.
- **CT Scan**: Intra-abdominal/pelvic abscess, anastomotic leak.

## Management

### Investigate & Treat the Cause

| Cause                      | Investigation                       | Treatment                                                   |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| `Atelectasis`              | Chest X-ray (rarely shows findings) | Incentive spirometry, ambulation, deep breathing exercises. |
| `UTI`                      | Urinalysis, culture                 | Remove/replace catheter, start antibiotics.                 |
| `Wound Infection`          | Wound culture                       | Incision & drainage, empirical antibiotics.                 |
| `DVT/PE`                   | Doppler US, CT angiography (for PE) | Anticoagulation.                                            |
| `Abscess/Anastomotic Leak` | CT scan                             | Drainage, IV antibiotics, surgery if needed.                |

### Supportive Measures

- Early ambulation → Prevents DVT, pneumonia.
- Pain control → Avoids hypoventilation (reduces atelectasis risk).
- Fluid management → Prevents dehydration, electrolyte imbalance.

## Prevention

- **Pre-op**: Optimize patient comorbidities (DM, smoking, obesity).
- **Intra-op**: Minimize blood loss, maintain asepsis, limit surgical time.
- **Post-op**:
  - Early mobilization (DVT, atelectasis).
  - Incentive spirometry (pneumonia prevention).
  - Proper wound care (infection control).
  - Remove catheters, drains early when possible.
