# Gangrene

> Gangrene is `necrosis of tissue` due to `ischemia` or `infection`.

It commonly affects extremities but can occur in organs.

### 1. Dry Gangrene

> Chronic ischemia due to arterial obstruction (e.g., atherosclerosis, diabetes).

- **Pathophysiology**:
  - Ischemia → tissue dries up & shrinks → black, mummified appearance.
  - No infection initially.
  - Demarcation line separates dead and viable tissue.
- **Common sites**: Toes, feet, fingers (especially in diabetics & smokers).

### 2. Wet Gangrene

> Superimposed bacterial infection on ischemic tissue.

- **Pathophysiology**:
  - Tissue death + infection → rapid putrefaction & swelling.
  - Foul-smelling, purulent discharge, edema.
  - Spreads fast → risk of septic shock.
- **Common sites**: Lower limbs, bowel (strangulated hernia, volvulus).

### 3. Gas Gangrene

> Clostridial infection (_Clostridium perfringens_).

- **Pathophysiology**:
  - Deep muscle wound → anaerobic conditions → _Clostridium_ produces exotoxins (α-toxin).
  - Rapid tissue destruction, gas bubbles (crepitus) in soft tissue.
  - Foul odor, severe pain, rapid spread.
- **Common sites**: War wounds, surgical wounds, compound fractures.

### 4. Fournier's Gangrene (Perineal Gangren)

> Polymicrobial necrotizing fasciitis of perineum.

- **Pathophysiology**:
  - Starts in scrotum, penis, perineum → spreads rapidly.
  - Severe pain, black necrotic tissue, foul smell.
  - High mortality without early treatment.

## Investigation

> Primarily by clinical diagnosis<br/>To confirm the type, identify the cause, assess the extent, and guide treatment

### Laboratory Investigations

1. **Complete Blood Count (CBC):**
   - ↑ Leukocytosis (suggests infection in wet/gas gangrene).
1. **C-Reactive Protein (CRP) & ESR:**
   - Elevated in infected or inflammatory gangrene.
1. **Blood Culture:**
   - To detect bacteremia/sepsis, especially in wet or gas gangrene.
1. **Lactate & Procalcitonin:**
   - ↑ Levels indicate sepsis or tissue hypoxia.
1. **Arterial Blood Gas (ABG):**
   - Metabolic acidosis (late sign of shock/sepsis).
1. **Serum Creatine Kinase (CK):**
   - Elevated in gas gangrene (muscle necrosis).

### Microbiological Study

1. **Gram Stain & Culture (Tissue/Pus):**
   - Identifies bacterial cause (e.g., _Clostridium perfringens_ in gas gangrene).
1. **Anaerobic Culture:**
   - Needed for suspected Clostridial gangrene.

### Imaging Studies

1. **X-ray (Plain Radiograph)**
   - Gas Gangrene: Shows **gas bubbles** in soft tissue (`subcutaneous emphysema`).
   - Chronic Ischemia (Dry Gangrene): May show vascular calcification.
1. **Ultrasound (Doppler)**
   - Evaluates **vascular supply** → detects **arterial occlusion**.
   - Useful in **dry gangrene** due to peripheral arterial disease.
1. **CT Scan**
   - Gas Gangrene:
     - Soft tissue edema, gas in muscles
     - Helps distinguish from necrotizing fasciitis
   - Vascular Assessment:
     - Detects **arterial obstruction/thrombosis**.
1. **MRI**
   - Best for soft tissue assessment
   - Detects necrotizing infection spread (e.g., in Fournier's gangrene).
1. **Angiography (Gold Standard for Ischemic Gangrene)**
   - Used in dry gangrene or Buerger's disease
   - Identifies **vascular blockages & collateral circulation**.

## Management

### 1. Dry Gangrene [#MX]

- **Revascularization** (if viable tissue remains):
  - `Angioplasty` or `bypass surgery` if arterial blockage is treatable.
- **Amputation**: If necrotic tissue is **non-viable**.
- **Avoid debridement** until **clear demarcation** forms.
- **Control risk factors** (diabetes, smoking, hypertension).

### 2. Wet Gangrene [#MX]

- **Urgent surgical debridement**.
- **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics** (`Piperacillin-tazobactam + Clindamycin`).
- **Amputation** if necrosis spreads rapidly.
- **Fluid resuscitation & sepsis management**.

### 3. Gas Gangrene [#MX]

- **Immediate debridement or amputation**.
- `IV Penicillin G + Clindamycin`.
- `Hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBO)` to inhibit _Clostridium_ growth.
- **Supportive care** for shock.

### 4. Fournier's Gangrene [#MX]

- **Aggressive surgical debridement** (repeat if needed).
- `IV Carbapenems + Clindamycin + Vancomycin`.
- **Colostomy** if anorectal involvement.
- **Skin grafting** after infection control.

## Buerger's Disease (Thromboangiitis Obliterans)

> `Buerger's disease` is a **non-atherosclerotic, inflammatory occlusive vasculitis** affecting **small and medium arteries & veins**, mostly in **young male smokers**.

<details>
<summary>Pathophysiology</summary>

1. **Tobacco use** triggers **immune-mediated vascular inflammation**.
1. **Inflammation → thrombosis** (clot formation) → **occlusion** of arteries.
1. **Tissue ischemia** → **gangrene of fingers & toes**.
1. **Superficial thrombophlebitis** and **Raynaud's phenomenon** may occur.

</details>

### Clinical Features

- **Intermittent claudication** (pain in hands/feet with activity).
- **Rest pain in severe cases**.
- **Cold sensitivity (Raynaud's-like symptoms)**.
- **Ulcers or gangrene in fingers/toes**.
- **Migratory superficial thrombophlebitis** (red, tender veins).

### Diagnosis

- **Clinical criteria (Shionoya's Criteria)**:
  - Age < 50 years.
  - History of smoking.
  - Distal extremity ischemia (claudication, gangrene).
  - No other arterial occlusive diseases (e.g., atherosclerosis).
  - No hypercoagulable state.
- **Doppler ultrasound & Angiography**:
  - Shows **"corkscrew collaterals"** due to compensatory vessels.

### Management of Buerger's Disease

1. Smoking Cessation (Most Important)
1. **Vasodilators**: `Iloprost` (Prostacyclin analog), `Calcium channel blockers` (Nifedipine) for Raynaud's symptoms
1. **Antiplatelets** (`Aspirin`, `Clopidogrel`) to reduce clotting risk.
1. Pain & Ulcer Management
   - `Analgesics` (NSAIDs, opioids if severe).
   - **Wound care & debridement** for ulcers.
1. Amputation (For Gangrene)
