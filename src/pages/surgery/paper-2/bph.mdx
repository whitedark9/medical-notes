# Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia

## Causes of Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)

> Acute urinary retention (AUR) is the **sudden inability to pass urine**, leading to painful bladder distension.

### Causes of Acute Urinary Retention

### 1. Obstructive Causes

1. **Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)**: most common in older males
1. **Urethral stricture**: due to infection, trauma, or instrumentation
1. **Bladder neck obstruction**: post-surgical scarring, congenital defects
1. **Prostate cancer**: advanced cases causing outflow obstruction
1. **Bladder stones**: blocking the bladder outlet
1. **Urethral calculus**: sudden blockage in the urethra
1. **Pelvic masses**: tumors compressing the bladder or urethra

### 2. Neurological Causes

1. **Spinal cord injury**: affects detrusor muscle control
1. **Multiple sclerosis**: demyelination affects bladder control
1. **Diabetic neuropathy**: damages autonomic control of urination
1. **Guillain-Barré syndrome**: acute paralysis affecting bladder function
1. **Parkinson's disease**: autonomic dysfunction affecting bladder emptying

### 3. Infective & Inflammatory Causes

1. **Urinary tract infection (UTI)**: severe inflammation and edema
1. **Prostatitis**: acute bacterial prostatitis causing swelling and obstruction
1. **Urethritis**: gonococcal or non-gonococcal infections
1. **Cystitis**: severe bladder inflammation causing detrusor dysfunction

### 4. Pharmacological Causes

(Medications causing urinary retention by affecting detrusor muscle or sphincter tone)

1. **Anticholinergics**: atropine, antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants
1. **Opioids**: morphine, codeine, fentanyl
1. **Alpha-adrenergic agonists**: pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine
1. **Calcium channel blockers**: verapamil, diltiazem
1. **NSAIDs**: ibuprofen, naproxen (rare)

### 5. Postoperative & Traumatic Causes

1. **Postoperative retention**: after spinal/epidural anesthesia, pelvic surgery
1. **Pelvic trauma**: urethral or bladder injury
1. **Prolonged catheterization**: detrusor dysfunction after removal

### 6. Functional & Psychogenic Causes

1. **Fowler's syndrome**: dysfunction of the urethral sphincter in young women
1. **Severe anxiety**: inhibits voluntary micturition reflex

### 7. Congenital Causes (Rare)

1. **Posterior urethral valves**: in newborn boys
1. **Meatal stenosis**: narrowing of the external urethral opening

## Clinical Features

### 1. Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms (LUTS)

1. **Storage symptoms (Irritative)**
   - Increased urinary frequency
   - Nocturia
   - Urgency
   - Urge incontinence (occasional)
1. **Voiding symptoms (Obstructive)**
   - Hesitancy
   - Weak urine stream
   - Straining to void
   - Intermittency (stopping and starting urine flow)
   - Sensation of incomplete emptying
   - Post-void dribbling
1. **Complications**
   - Acute urinary retention (AUR) - painful inability to pass urine
   - Chronic urinary retention - painless retention with overflow incontinence
   - Recurrent UTIs - due to incomplete bladder emptying
   - Bladder stones - from urinary stasis
   - Hydronephrosis & renal failure - in severe cases

## Investigations

### 1. Laboratory Investigations

1. **Urinalysis** - To rule out UTI, hematuria, or glycosuria
1. **Serum creatinine & eGFR** - To assess renal function (if suspecting obstruction)
1. **Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA)** - Elevated in BPH, prostatitis, or prostate cancer

### 2. Imaging

1. **Ultrasound (USG) KUB + Post-Void Residual (PVR)**
   - Assesses prostate size and residual urine volume
   - Detects hydronephrosis (if chronic retention)
1. **Transrectal Ultrasound (TRUS)**
   - More accurate prostate volume measurement
   - Used if considering prostate biopsy (e.g., elevated PSA)

### 3. Urodynamic Studies (if diagnosis is unclear)

1. **Uroflowmetry** - Reduced peak urinary flow rate (< 10 mL/s suggests obstruction)
1. **Pressure-flow studies** - Confirms bladder outlet obstruction

### 4. Cystoscopy (if indicated)

- Performed in patients with hematuria, suspected stricture, or bladder stones

### 1. Conservative Management

- **Lifestyle modifications**
  - Reduce evening fluid intake
  - Avoid caffeine & alcohol (reduce bladder irritation)
  - Double voiding (urinate, then try again after a few minutes)
  - Bladder training exercises
- **Watchful waiting**
  - For mild symptoms (IPSS**< 8**) and no complications
  - Regular follow-up with symptom assessment

### 2. Medical Management

> For moderate symptoms (IPSS 8-19) without complications

#### A. Alpha-1 Adrenergic Blockers (First-line)

- **Drugs**: `Tamsulosin`, `Alfuzosin`, `Doxazosin`
- **Mechanism**: Relaxes smooth muscle of prostate & bladder neck → Improves urine flow
- **Side Effects**: Postural hypotension, dizziness, retrograde ejaculation

#### B. 5α-Reductase Inhibitors

- **Drugs**: `Finasteride`, `Dutasteride`
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits testosterone conversion → Shrinks prostate (effective for large prostates >40 mL)
- **Side Effects**: Decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, gynecomastia

#### C. Combination Therapy

- `Tamsulosin` + `Finasteride` (For severe symptoms, large prostate)

#### D. Other Medications

- **Phosphodiesterase-5 Inhibitors (`Tadalafil` 5mg/day)**: If BPH coexists with erectile dysfunction
- **Anticholinergics (`Oxybutynin`, `Solifenacin`)**: For predominant storage symptoms (use cautiously to avoid retention)

### 3. Surgical Management

#### Indications

1. **Failed medical treatment**
1. **Acute urinary retention (AUR) requiring repeated catheterization**
1. **Recurrent urinary tract infections**
1. **Bladder stones** due to urinary stasis
1. **Recurrent hematuria** due to BPH
1. **Chronic urinary retention** with high post-void residual (>300 mL)
1. **Renal insufficiency** due to bladder outlet obstruction

##### A. Transurethral Resection of the Prostate (TURP) _(Gold standard)_

- **Indications**: Moderate to severe BPH, prostate size < 80 mL
- **Procedure**:
  - Resectoscope inserted via urethra
  - Prostate tissue resected with electrocautery
  - Continuous irrigation to prevent clot formation
- **Post-op Complications**:
  - **Early**: Bleeding, clot retention, UTI, TUR syndrome (hyponatremia from irrigation fluid absorption)
  - **Late**: Retrograde ejaculation, urethral stricture, incontinence

##### B. Open Simple Prostatectomy _(For very large prostates >80-100 mL)_

- **Types**: Retropubic (Millin's), Suprapubic (Freyer's)
- **Procedure**: Prostate adenoma removed via abdominal incision
- **Complications**: Bleeding, infection, erectile dysfunction

##### C. Minimally Invasive Procedures

- **Holmium Laser Enucleation of the Prostate (HoLEP)**
  - Laser removes obstructing prostate tissue
  - Less bleeding, suitable for large prostates
- **Photoselective Vaporization of the Prostate (PVP)**
  - Laser vaporizes prostate tissue
  - Less invasive, lower bleeding risk
- **Transurethral Incision of the Prostate (TUIP)**
  - Incisions made at bladder neck → Reduces resistance
  - For small prostates with significant obstruction

##### D. Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE) _(For patients unfit for surgery)_

- **Mechanism**: Embolization reduces blood supply → Shrinks prostate

#### Preoperative Preparation

1. **Assess for surgical fitness**: ECG, renal function, coagulation profile
2. **Catheterization**: If in retention, to decompress bladder
3. **Antibiotic prophylaxis**: To prevent post-op UTI
4. **Stop anticoagulants/antiplatelets**: To reduce bleeding risk
5. **Bowel preparation**: If open surgery planned

#### Postoperative

##### Immediate Post-op Care

- **Continuous bladder irrigation (CBI)**: Prevents clot formation
- **Monitor urine output**: Ensure adequate drainage, look for hematuria
- **Pain control**: IV analgesia initially
- **Remove catheter (after 24-48 hours for TURP, 5-7 days for open surgery)**

##### Post-op Complications

1. Early
   - **Bleeding**: Can cause clot retention (manage with irrigation, transfusion if severe)
   - **TUR Syndrome**: Hyponatremia from excessive irrigation fluid absorption (nausea, confusion, seizures)
   - **Infection (UTI, prostatitis)**
1. Late
   - **Retrograde ejaculation** (most common): Sperm enters bladder instead of urethra
   - **Urethral stricture**: Due to scarring from instrumentation
   - **Incontinence**: Rare, usually temporary
   - **Erectile dysfunction**: Less common than with open prostatectomy
