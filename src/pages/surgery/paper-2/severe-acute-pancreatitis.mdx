# Severe Acute Pancreatitis (SAP)

> Severe acute pancreatitis is an acute inflammation of the pancreas associated with **organ failure**, **local or systemic complications**, and/or **necrosis**, often requiring intensive care.

## Causes

> I GET SMASHED

1. `I`diopathic
1. `G`allstones (most common)
1. `E`thanol (alcohol abuse)
1. `T`rauma
1. `S`teroids
1. `M`umps and other infections (e.g., Coxsackie virus)
1. `A`utoimmune (autoimmune pancreatitis)
1. `S`corpion sting (rare)
1. `H`ypercalcemia, Hyperlipidemia
1. `E`RCP (iatrogenic)
1. `D`rugs (azathioprine, thiazides, valproate, etc.)

## Pathology

### Pathophysiology

**Trigger (e.g., gallstone, alcohol) → pancreatic acinar cell injury → premature activation of trypsinogen to trypsin inside pancreas**
→ autodigestion of pancreatic tissue
→ local inflammation, necrosis, and hemorrhage
→ release of cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)
→ **SIRS (systemic inflammatory response syndrome)**
→ multiorgan dysfunction (lung, kidney, cardiovascular system)

### Types of Morphological Changes

- **Interstitial edematous pancreatitis**: mild, self-limiting
- **Necrotizing pancreatitis**: necrosis of parenchyma and/or peripancreatic tissue
- May lead to **infected necrosis**, **pancreatic abscess**, or **pseudocyst**

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

- Sudden, severe **epigastric pain** radiating to back
- **Nausea, vomiting**
- Aggravated by eating; relieved by sitting forward

### Signs

- **Fever**, **tachycardia**
- **Abdominal tenderness**, guarding
- **Hypotension** (due to third-spacing of fluid)
- **Grey-Turner's sign**: flank ecchymosis (retroperitoneal hemorrhage)
- **Cullen's sign**: periumbilical ecchymosis
- **Ileus** (absent bowel sounds)

### Signs of Severe Disease

- Hypoxia
- Oliguria/acute kidney injury
- Altered mental status
- Hypocalcemia (→ tetany)
- Persistent hypotension or shock

## Investigations

### Laboratory

- `↑ Serum amylase/lipase` (> 3x normal)
- `↑ WBC, CRP, procalcitonin`
- `↓ Calcium`
- `LFTs` (↑ ALT suggests gallstone pancreatitis)
- `ABG`: may show hypoxia or metabolic acidosis
- `BUN/Creatinine`: for AKI
- `Glucose`: hyperglycemia

### Imaging

- **CT abdomen with contrast**: for necrosis, pseudocyst, fluid collections
- **Ultrasound**: detect gallstones, biliary obstruction
- **MRCP/ERCP**: if biliary cause suspected

## Management

### Emergency Management

#### A – Airway

- Ensure patency; may require intubation if GCS ↓ or respiratory failure (ARDS)

#### B – Breathing

- O₂ supplementation
- Monitor for **ARDS** (acute respiratory distress syndrome)
- ABG if hypoxic or respiratory distress

#### C – Circulation

- **Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation** with crystalloids (e.g. Ringer's lactate)
  - Goal: urine output > 0.5 mL/kg/hr, MAP > 65 mmHg
- Monitor for signs of shock
- Vasopressors if hypotension persists despite fluids (in ICU setting)

#### D – Disability

- Monitor GCS
- Pain control: IV opioids (e.g. `morphine` or `fentanyl`)

#### E – Exposure

- Look for signs of **ecchymosis** (Cullen's sign, Grey-Turner’s sign)\*
- Evaluate for **compartment syndrome**

#### F – Fluids (ongoing)

- Fluid balance monitoring
- Catheterize if needed for accurate urine output
- Repeat hematocrit, BUN to guide fluid therapy

---

### Supportive Therapy

- **NPO initially**: until vomiting settles or ileus resolves
- Early **enteral nutrition** via NG/ND tube within 72 hrs (↓ risk of infection)
- **IV PPI** if risk of stress ulcers
- **Glycemic control**
- **Antibiotics:** _not routine_, only if:
  - Infected pancreatic necrosis (confirmed by gas on CT or positive cultures)
  - Other infections (e.g. UTI, cholangitis, pneumonia)

---

### Management of Complications

#### Pancreatic necrosis

- **Sterile necrosis**: supportive care
- **Infected necrosis**:
  - CT-guided fine-needle aspiration (FNA) to confirm
  - Start antibiotics (`carbapenems` or `quinolone + metronidazole`)
  - **Minimally invasive necrosectomy** (step-up approach) if not improving

#### Pseudocyst (>4 weeks)

- Percutaneous, endoscopic, or surgical drainage if:
  - Symptomatic
  - \>6 cm or infected

#### Ascites, pleural effusion

- Drain if large or causing symptoms

#### Multi-organ failure (MOF)

- ICU support
- Mechanical ventilation, dialysis as needed

#### Gallstone pancreatitis

- ERCP with sphincterotomy **within 24-72 hrs** if cholangitis/CBD stone present
- Elective **cholecystectomy after recovery** to prevent recurrence

## Prognosis

- Mortality:
  - \~3–5% in mild cases
  - Up to 30–50% in severe cases with infected necrosis or MOF
- **Prognostic scores**:
  - **Modified Marshall Score** (organ failure)
  - **Ranson’s criteria**
  - **APACHE II**
  - **BISAP score**
