# Urolithiasis (Urinary Stones)

## Causes

### 1. Metabolic Causes

1. **Hypercalciuria** → Increased urinary calcium excretion (most common)
   - Primary hyperparathyroidism
   - Sarcoidosis, Vitamin D toxicity
   - High dietary calcium intake
1. **Hyperoxaluria** → Increased oxalate excretion
   - Excess dietary oxalate (spinach, nuts, chocolate)
   - Malabsorption (Crohn's disease, short bowel syndrome)
1. **Hyperuricosuria** → Increased uric acid in urine
   - Gout, high purine diet (red meat, seafood)
1. **Hypocitraturia** → Decreased urinary citrate (citrate prevents st one formation)
   - Chronic metabolic acidosis (renal tubular acidosis, diarrhea)

### 2. Infectious Causes

- **Struvite stones (Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate stones)**
  - Caused by **urease-producing bacteria** (Proteus, Klebsiella)
  - Leads to **alkaline urine** → stone formation

### 3. Structural & Functional Causes

- **Urinary stasis** → Promotes stone formation
  - **Obstruction** (e.g., ureteropelvic junction obstruction, BPH)
  - **Neurogenic bladder**
- **Medullary sponge kidney** → Congenital cystic dilation of renal tubules

### 4. Drug-Induced Causes

- **Drugs that crystallize in urine** → `Acyclovir`, `Indinavir`, `Sulfonamides`, `Triamterene`

### 5. Dietary & Lifestyle Factors

- **Low fluid intake** → Concentrated urine promotes stone formation
- **High sodium intake** → Increases calcium excretion
- **High oxalate diet** → Increases oxalate levels (spinach, rhubarb, tea)

## Clinical Features

### 1. Renal Colic (Severe Flank Pain)

- **Sudden, severe, colicky pain**
- **Location of pain depends on stone position:**
  - **Upper ureter/kidney** → **Flank pain**
  - **Mid-ureter** → **Pain radiating to lower abdomen**
  - **Lower ureter** → **Groin/testicular/labial pain**

### 2. Hematuria

- **Microscopic or gross hematuria**
- Due to **mucosal injury** from stone movement

### 3. Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms (LUTS)

- **Dysuria, urgency, frequency** → If stone is near bladder
- **Urinary retention** → If obstructing urethra

### 4. Signs of Complications

- **Fever, chills, pyuria** → Suggests **infected stone (urolithiasis with UTI)**
- **Hydronephrosis** → If **prolonged obstruction**
- **Acute kidney injury** → If **bilateral obstruction or single functioning kidney**

## Investigations for Urolithiasis

### 1. Laboratory Investigations

- **Urinalysis** → Hematuria, crystals, infection (WBCs, nitrites)
- **Urine pH** → Helps identify stone type:
  - **Acidic (pH < 5.5)** → Uric acid, cystine stones
  - **Alkaline (pH >7.0)** → Struvite stones (infection stones)
- **24-hour urine analysis** → Assesses calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid
- **Serum tests**
  - **Calcium** → Hyperparathyroidism, hypercalcemia
  - **Uric acid** → Gout-related stones
  - **Creatinine, eGFR** → Renal function assessment

### 2. Imaging Investigations

- **Non-contrast CT KUB (Kidneys, Ureters, Bladder)** _(Gold standard)_
  - Detects stones, size, location, obstruction
- **Ultrasound KUB**
  - Preferred in **pregnant women & children**
  - Good for detecting renal stones, hydronephrosis
  - May miss small ureteric stones
- **X-ray KUB**
  - Detects **radiopaque stones (calcium, struvite stones)**
  - **Cannot detect** uric acid or cystine stones
- **IV Urography (IVU)** _(Rarely used today)_
  - Previously used to assess obstruction before CT availability

## Management

### 1. General Principles

> Stone size & location determine management approach

- **Small stones (< 5 mm)** → Usually pass spontaneously
- **Large stones (>10 mm), symptomatic, or complicated stones** → Require intervention

### 2. Acute Management (Emergency Situations)

Indications for **urgent intervention**:

- **Obstructed infected stone (Urosepsis)** → Fever, tachycardia, pyuria
- **Acute urinary retention** (bilateral obstruction or single kidney)
- **Uncontrolled pain, vomiting, dehydration**

### Emergency Measures

- **A - Airway, B - Breathing, C - Circulation** → IV fluids, oxygen if septic
- **D - Disability (Pain Control)**
  - **NSAIDs (First-line)** - `Diclofenac`, `Ibuprofen`
  - **Opioids (if severe pain)** - `Morphine`
- **E - Exposure & Electrolytes**
  - Check **serum creatinine, urea, electrolytes**
- **F - Fluid Management**
  - IV fluids only **if no obstruction** (Excess fluid can worsen hydronephrosis)
  - **Obstruction + infection → Urgent decompression**
    - **Percutaneous nephrostomy (if proximal obstruction)**
    - **Ureteric stent placement (if distal obstruction)**

### 3. Conservative Management (For Small Stones < 5 mm)

- **Fluids** → Encourage intake of **2.5-3 L/day**
- **Pain relief** → NSAIDs (e.g., `Diclofenac, Ibuprofen`)
- **Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET)** (For ureteric stones)
  - `Tamsulosin` (α1-blocker) → Relaxes ureter smooth muscle
  - `Nifedipine` (Calcium channel blocker, less common)
- **Follow-up**
  - Serial imaging (USG/CT KUB) to ensure passage
  - Strain urine to collect stone for analysis

### 4. Surgical Management (For Large or Symptomatic Stones)

#### Indications for Surgery

- **Stone >10 mm**
- **Failed medical treatment** (No passage in 4-6 weeks)
- **Recurrent UTIs or hydronephrosis**
- **Severe pain despite analgesia**
- **Renal function deterioration**

#### Surgical Procedures

##### A. Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy (ESWL)

- **Indication**: Stones **< 20 mm**, best for renal stones
- **Procedure**: High-energy shock waves break stone → Passed in urine
- **Contraindications**: Pregnancy, bleeding disorders, obesity
- **Complications**: Hematuria, incomplete stone clearance

##### B. Ureteroscopy (URS) ± Laser Lithotripsy

- **Indication**: Ureteric stones **< 10 mm**
- **Procedure**: Endoscope inserted via urethra → Laser fragmentation
- **Complications**: Ureteric injury, stricture formation

##### C. Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy (PCNL)

- **Indication**: Large renal stones (>20 mm), stag-horn calculi
- **Procedure**: **Needle inserted into kidney → Stone extracted or fragmented**
- **Complications**: Bleeding, infection, renal injury

##### D. Open or Laparoscopic Stone Removal (Rare)

- **Indication**: Complex stones, failed other methods
- **Types**: **Pyelolithotomy, Ureterolithotomy, Nephrolithotomy**

### 5. Prevention of Recurrence

- **High fluid intake** → At least **2.5 L/day** to dilute urine
- **Dietary modifications**
  - `Calcium stones` → Avoid excess sodium, limit oxalate-rich foods
  - `Uric acid stones` → Low purine diet (avoid red meat, seafood)
  - `Struvite stones` → Target urease-producing bacteria (`ciprofloxacin`, `trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole`)
  - `Cystine stones` → Alkalinize urine (Potassium citrate, Acetazolamide)
- **Medications** (If metabolic disorder detected)
  - `Thiazide diuretics` → Reduce calcium excretion
  - `Allopurinol` → For uric acid stones in hyperuricemia
