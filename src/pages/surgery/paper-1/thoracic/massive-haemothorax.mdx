# Massive Haemothorax

> Accumulation of **>1500 mL blood** in the pleural space or **>1/3 of circulating blood volume**, usually due to **blunt or penetrating thoracic trauma**.

> Life-threatening and requires **rapid recognition and intervention**!!!

## Diagnosis

### History

- Recent **chest trauma** (blunt or penetrating)
- Sudden onset **dyspnoea** and **chest pain**
- Symptoms of **hypovolaemia**: dizziness, fainting, palpitations

### Examination

- **Inspection**: pallor, anxiety, signs of shock, asymmetric chest expansion
- **Palpation**: reduced chest expansion on affected side
- **Percussion**: dullness over fluid-filled hemithorax
- **Auscultation**: absent or markedly reduced breath sounds on affected side
- **Signs of shock**: tachycardia, hypotension, cool extremities

### Investigations (If not diagnosed clinically)

- **Chest X-ray** (AP supine or erect if possible):

  - Homogeneous opacity on affected side
  - Contralateral mediastinal shift if massive

- **Ultrasound (FAST scan)**: rapid detection of pleural fluid
- **CT Thorax**: detailed evaluation if patient stable and diagnosis uncertain
- **Blood tests**: CBC, type & crossmatch, coagulation profile, ABG

## Management

### Immediate / Emergency

1. **Resuscitation**

   - Establish **two large-bore IV lines** or **intraosseous access**
   - Begin rapid infusion of **crystalloids**
   - Early **blood transfusion** if ongoing blood loss suspected

2. **Chest Tube Thoracostomy**

   - Insert **large-bore tube** (28–36 Fr) in **5th intercostal space, anterior/mid-axillary line**
   - Drain initial volume: >1500 mL confirms **massive haemothorax**
   - Continuous monitoring of output

3. **Emergency Thoracotomy Indications**

   - Immediate thoracotomy if:

     - **>1500 mL initial chest tube output**
     - **>200 mL/hour ongoing output** for 2–4 hours
     - Persistent shock despite resuscitation

### Supportive Measures

- Oxygen therapy
- Analgesia (`paracetamol`, `NSAIDs`, opioids if needed)
- Monitor vital signs, urine output, and oxygenation

### Definitive / Surgical Care

- **Control bleeding source** (intercostal vessel, lung parenchyma, heart, great vessels)
- **Repair associated injuries** (diaphragm, lung laceration)
- Postoperative chest physiotherapy to prevent atelectasis
